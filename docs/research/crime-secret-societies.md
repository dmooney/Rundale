# Crime & Secret Societies in 1820s Ireland

## Research Document — Rundale (Kilteevan, County Roscommon)

### Agrarian Secret Societies: Origins and Local Variants

The Irish countryside in the 1820s was governed by two overlapping systems of law: the formal apparatus of the British crown, and the informal, often violent enforcement of communal norms by **agrarian secret societies**. These organizations were not revolutionary movements in any modern sense. They did not seek to overthrow the state or establish a republic. Their aims were intensely local — to regulate rents, prevent evictions, control the price of labor, resist tithes paid to the established Protestant church, and punish those who violated the unwritten customs that governed access to land. Understanding these societies is essential to understanding rural Ireland in this period, because their influence touched nearly every parish, and Kilteevan in County Roscommon was no exception.

The original model for agrarian agitation was the **Whiteboys**, who first appeared in County Tipperary in late 1761 and quickly spread across Munster. [1] They took their name from the white smocks worn over their clothes during nighttime raids. The Whiteboys attacked enclosures of common land, tore down fences, maimed cattle belonging to offending landlords [11], and sent **threatening letters** — often elaborately worded and signed with pseudonyms like "Queen Sive" (also known as "Sive Oultagh") [2] — to tithe proctors and land agents. Although the original Whiteboy movement was suppressed, the name became a generic term. Well into the 1820s, magistrates and newspapers referred to any agrarian agitators as "Whiteboys" regardless of their actual affiliation. The pattern the Whiteboys established — anonymous collective action, theatrical costumes, threatening notices, escalating violence against property before turning to persons — persisted for decades and was replicated by every subsequent society.

Of direct relevance to Roscommon and the wider province of Connacht were the **Threshers**, active in the early 1800s (notably from around 1806–1807). [3] The Threshers were particularly strong in counties Sligo, Leitrim, Mayo, and Roscommon, making them the society most likely to have had a living presence in the memories of Kilteevan's older residents during the 1820s. Their primary grievance was the **tithe system**, which compelled Catholic farmers to pay a tax — often collected with aggressive zeal by tithe proctors — to support the Church of Ireland, a church they did not attend and whose clergy they regarded as alien. The Threshers administered **oaths** to recruits, binding them to secrecy and mutual aid. They attacked tithe proctors, intimidated farmers who cooperated with tithe collection, and occasionally turned their attention to priests whom they felt charged excessive fees for marriages, baptisms, and funerals. Their name likely derived from the flails used in threshing grain, a common farm implement that doubled as a weapon, though one contemporary source links the name to their practice of "threshing" (flogging) victims. [4]

By the 1820s, the **Ribbonmen** had emerged as the most widespread secret society in Ulster and parts of Leinster and Connacht. [5] The Ribbon movement was more explicitly **sectarian** than the Whiteboys or Threshers — it defined itself in opposition to the **Orange Order** and framed its activities partly in terms of Catholic solidarity against Protestant ascendancy. Yet the Ribbonmen were also deeply agrarian in their concerns, and their lodges served as mutual-aid networks for laborers and smallholders. Ribbon lodges were organized hierarchically, with local chapters answering to county and provincial boards, and members were bound by elaborate **oaths of secrecy**. The oath was no mere formality. It was understood as a sacred and terrifying commitment. A man who took the Ribbon oath placed himself under the society's discipline for life, and breaking it — whether through betrayal or simple failure to act when called upon — could result in punishment up to and including death.

In Munster during the early 1820s (1821–1824), the **Rockites** conducted a particularly intense campaign of agrarian violence. [6] Named after the pseudonymous "Captain Rock" who signed their threatening letters, the Rockites attacked landlords, agents, tithe proctors, and anyone perceived as collaborating with the authorities. Their methods included **burning haystacks**, destroying crops, and the **maiming of livestock** — a practice that struck at the economic foundations of farming communities and carried enormous symbolic weight. To hamstring a man's cow or cut the ears off his horse was to declare him an outcast, marked by the community's judgment. Though centered on Munster, Rockite-style agitation influenced secret-society activity across Ireland.

The **Terry Alts**, founded in 1828 in the Corofin/Kilnaboy area of County Clare and active until around 1831, followed the same essential pattern but with particular ferocity. [7] They took their name from a fictional leader, "Terry Alt," and focused on preventing the consolidation of smallholdings — the practice by which landlords evicted multiple tenants to create larger, more profitable farms. The Terry Alts targeted **land-grabbers**, the term used for anyone who took possession of land from which another tenant had been evicted. To be branded a land-grabber was to become a social pariah, and the Terry Alts enforced this stigma with arson, cattle-maiming, and physical assault.

### Methods and Punishments

The methods employed by these societies followed a recognizable escalation. The first warning was typically a **threatening letter** or notice, posted on a chapel door or left at a person's home, written in formal and often florid language and signed by a fictitious captain. If the warning went unheeded, the next step was damage to property — **haystacks burned**, fences torn down, crops trampled. After that came attacks on livestock, which were deeply distressing in a society where a single cow might represent a family's entire capital. Finally, there was violence against the person: beatings, shootings, and the gruesome practice of **carding**, in which the victim was tortured by dragging a **wool-carding comb** — an iron implement with one or two rows of teeth, each several inches long, used to prepare raw wool for spinning — across their bare skin, tearing the flesh. [8] Carding was employed as a punishment for informers, land-grabbers, and those who defied the society's decrees. It was meant to be exemplary: the victim, scarred for life, served as a walking warning to others.

**Faction fighting** was a distinct but related phenomenon. At fairs, **pattern days** (pilgrimages to holy wells and local saints' sites), and other large gatherings, organized groups from rival townlands or families engaged in pitched battles using blackthorn sticks (shillelaghs), stones, and occasionally more lethal weapons. [9] These fights had deep roots in local rivalries and were governed by their own rough codes of honor. Faction fights were not always connected to secret societies, but the networks of loyalty and enmity they created overlapped with society membership, and a man's faction allegiance was part of his social identity.

Beyond organized agitation, everyday rural crime included **livestock theft and cattle-driving** (the forced removal of cattle from land deemed improperly held), **poitin distillation** (illegal whiskey production, ubiquitous in the west of Ireland), and the **smuggling** of tobacco and tea to avoid excise duties. Poitin-making was so widespread as to be nearly universal in parts of Connacht, and the excise men who attempted to suppress it were deeply unpopular. A still hidden in a bog or mountainside was a normal part of the rural economy, and its destruction by authorities was felt as an act of oppression.

### Crown Justice vs. Community Justice

The formal justice system — the **assizes**, the magistrates, the military garrisons — operated according to English law and was administered largely by the Protestant landowning class. For the Catholic majority, this system was foreign in language, religion, and sympathy. Punishments were severe: **transportation to the penal colonies in Australia** for terms of seven years, fourteen years, or life was the standard sentence for agrarian offences, and **hanging** was imposed for murder and for administering unlawful oaths. [10] **Flogging** was used as both judicial punishment and military discipline. The severity of these punishments did not deter secret-society activity so much as drive it further underground and intensify the culture of silence that protected it.

Against this stood the system of **community justice** — the unwritten rules, enforced by collective action, that governed who could hold land, what rents were acceptable, how laborers should be paid, and how the proceeds of the harvest should be distributed. This was not a formal legal code but a set of deeply held norms, and the secret societies were its enforcement arm. When a landlord evicted a tenant and installed another, the community did not appeal to the courts; it sent a threatening letter, and if that failed, it burned the new tenant's haystacks. When a farmer paid his laborers below the customary rate, he found his cattle maimed. This system was brutal, but it was understood by those who lived under it as legitimate in a way that the crown's justice was not.

The figure of the **informer** occupied a unique place of horror in this world. To inform on a neighbor to the authorities — to give evidence at the assizes, to identify members of a secret society, to cooperate with the magistrate — was the ultimate betrayal. Informers were subject to carding, assassination, and a social death that extended to their families. The word itself, in Irish rural culture, carried a weight comparable to the gravest obscenity. Even the suspicion of informing could destroy a person, and the fear of being branded an informer was one of the most powerful forces maintaining the silence that shielded the societies from prosecution.

### Game Design Implications

These historical realities suggest several rich systems for Rundale. **Secret society membership** should function as a hidden NPC attribute, influencing behavior, dialogue, and alliances in ways the player must discover through observation and conversation rather than through explicit labels. Some NPCs will be oath-bound Threshers or Ribbonmen; others will be sympathizers; still others will be opposed, whether out of loyalty to the crown, religious conviction, or simple fear. **Threatening letters** can appear as in-game events — posted on the chapel door, discovered at a player-allied NPC's home — serving as narrative triggers that force the player to choose sides or attempt mediation.

**Faction tensions** between rival townlands or families provide a persistent source of social conflict that operates independently of the main narrative. The player may witness or be drawn into faction fights at fairs and pattern days, and their responses will shape their reputation with different groups. **Moral choices** around crime — whether to report a poitin still, whether to shelter a man accused of cattle-maiming, whether to carry a threatening letter or refuse — should have no cleanly "good" outcome. The crown's justice is harsh and alien; the community's justice is brutal and sometimes unjust. The player is caught between systems, and every choice closes some doors while opening others.

**Crime as narrative driver** means that secret-society activity should not be mere background flavor but an active force generating quests, conflicts, and consequences. A campaign of tithe resistance in the parish can escalate from threatening letters to arson to murder, pulling the player deeper into a crisis where neutrality becomes impossible. The **informer dynamic** is especially potent: the player may be asked to inform, may be suspected of informing, or may need to identify an informer within the community, with life-and-death stakes on every side.

## Fact-Check Notes

[1] **Corrected.** The original text stated the Whiteboys "first appeared in County Tipperary and across Munster in the 1760s." More precisely, the first Whiteboy activity began in the closing months of 1761, in the district between Clogheen and Ballyporeen in County Tipperary, then spread to counties Limerick, Cork, and Waterford. The text has been revised to "late 1761." — Wikipedia, "Whiteboys"; Encyclopedia.com, "Whiteboys and Whiteboyism"; Cambridge Core, "The Whiteboy Movement, 1761–5."

[2] **Corrected.** The original text listed "Captain Right" as a Whiteboy pseudonym. "Captain Right" was actually associated with the later **Rightboys** movement of the 1780s, not the original Whiteboys. The Whiteboys used pseudonyms including "Queen Sive" (also "Sive Oultagh"), "Captain Firebrand," and "Captain Moonlight." The text has been revised to remove the incorrect attribution. — Wikipedia, "Rightboys"; Encyclopedia.com, "Whiteboys and Whiteboyism"; academia.edu, "Captain Right — The Rightboy Movement of the Late 18th Century."

[3] **Verified.** The Threshers were active in the early 1800s, particularly from around 1806–1807, in Connacht counties including Roscommon, Sligo, Leitrim, and Mayo. Their primary grievance was tithes and excessive clerical dues. — libraryireland.com, "Irish Secret Societies"; Simmons College LibGuide, "Pre-Famine Organizations."

[4] **Partially verified.** The common explanation links the name to threshing flails, but a contemporary source from 1806 states they were called "Threshers" from "their flogging the parties with briars and nettles." The flail explanation is widely cited in secondary sources and remains plausible given the naming conventions of agrarian societies. — libraryireland.com, "Irish Secret Societies"; Heritage History, "Secret Societies of All Ages."

[5] **Verified.** The Ribbonmen emerged from the Defender tradition in the early 19th century, with the movement becoming active around 1810 and gaining significant traction between 1816 and 1824. They were most prominent in Ulster, north Leinster, and north Connacht, and defined themselves primarily in opposition to the Orange Order. — Wikipedia, "Ribbonism"; Britannica, "Ribbonism"; neverfeltbetter.wordpress.com, "Ireland's Wars: Ribbonmen."

[6] **Verified.** The Rockite movement of 1821–1824 was centered on Munster, originating in western County Limerick. Named after the pseudonymous "Captain Rock," the Rockites killed over 100 people, and the state responded with over 100 executions and 600 transportations. James S. Donnelly Jr.'s *Captain Rock* (2009) is the definitive study. — Project MUSE; History Ireland; The Irish Story.

[7] **Verified with refinement.** The Terry Alts were founded in 1828 in the Corofin/Kilnaboy area of County Clare and were active until approximately 1831. They focused on preventing land consolidation and targeted "land-grabbers." The text originally said "late 1820s and early 1830s," which has been made more precise. — Wikipedia, "Terry Alts"; Clare Library; History Ireland, "The Terry Alt Movement 1829–31."

[8] **Verified.** Carding (also called "combing") is a form of torture using iron combs designed for processing wool. These instruments had one or two rows of iron teeth several inches long. The practice was used by Irish agrarian secret societies to punish informers and transgressors. The original text described the comb as "a board studded with iron teeth," which has been revised to more accurately describe the implement. — Wikipedia, "Combing (torture)"; liquisearch.com, "Carding (torture) — Iron Comb."

[9] **Verified.** Faction fighting was widespread at fairs and pattern days in 18th- and 19th-century Ireland. Combatants typically used shillelaghs (blackthorn sticks), stones, and improvised weapons. Pattern days, originally pilgrimages to holy wells, routinely evolved into gatherings with drinking, dancing, and faction fights. — Wikipedia, "Faction fighting in Ireland"; National Museum of Ireland, "19th Century Faction Fighting."

[10] **Verified.** Transportation to Australian penal colonies for terms of 7, 14 years, or life was the standard sentence for agrarian offences. Hanging was imposed for murder and for administering unlawful oaths. During the Rockite agitation alone, over 100 were executed and 600 transported. — Donnelly, *Captain Rock*; Encyclopedia.com, "Tithe War."

[11] **Verified.** The Whiteboys' initial activities included tearing down fences and hedges over common land, digging up pasture, and levelling enclosures. They also maimed cattle and attacked tithe proctors. — Wikipedia, "Whiteboys"; irishcentral.com, "The original rebels: Ireland's infamous Whiteboys."
