# Economy & Trade in 1820s Ireland

The economy of 1820s Ireland was an economy in distress. The end of the Napoleonic Wars in 1815 had pulled the floor out from under Irish agriculture, and the decade that followed was defined by falling prices, rising rents, desperate poverty, and a population that continued to grow even as the resources to sustain it shrank. Understanding how money moved — and how most people had almost none of it — is essential for building a world where economic anxiety shapes every interaction.

## The Post-War Crash

During the wars against Napoleon, Irish agriculture had boomed. British demand for grain, meat, butter, and provisions to feed armies and navies had pushed prices to extraordinary heights, and Irish farmers had expanded production to match.[1] Land that had been pasture was ploughed for tillage. Rents rose because landlords saw what the land could earn, and tenants paid because prices justified it. Then the wars ended. The **post-Napoleonic economic crash** hit Irish agriculture like a hammer.[2] Grain prices collapsed — wheat, which had fetched over 80 shillings a quarter during the war, fell to under 40 shillings by the early 1820s.[3] Oats and barley followed. Butter prices dropped. Cattle prices fell. But rents did not fall, or fell far less than prices. Landlords had set their expectations during the boom and were reluctant — or financially unable — to lower them. Tenants who had taken leases at wartime rates now found themselves locked into payments they could not sustain from what the land earned. The result was a decade of grinding deflation, mounting arrears, and a constant, pervasive fear of eviction.

## Subsistence and Market Agriculture

Most small farmers in 1820s Ireland lived in a **dual economy**. They grew **potatoes** to feed their families — the potato was the subsistence base, the crop that kept body and soul together on half an acre or less. Everything else they grew was for the market, not the table. A smallholder might plant oats or barley on his remaining ground, harvest it, and sell it to pay the rent. He might keep a pig, fatten it on scraps and whey, and sell it at the local fair. He might have a cow whose butter went to market. The brutal arithmetic of this system was that a family could be simultaneously feeding itself adequately on potatoes and selling everything else it produced just to hold onto the land. In a bad year — a poor harvest, a drop in prices, sickness in the family — the margin vanished entirely, and the choice became stark: pay the rent or eat the seed corn. This is not metaphorical poverty. It was the lived reality for the majority of the rural population.

## Rent, Middlemen, and the Threat of Eviction

The **rent system** was the engine that drove the rural economy and the mechanism that extracted its surplus. Nearly all agricultural land in Ireland was owned by **Protestant Ascendancy landlords**, and tenants paid **cash rents** for the right to work it. In theory, this was a straightforward arrangement. In practice, it was layered with exploitation. Many landlords were **absentee**, living in England, and managed their estates through **land agents** who were incentivised to maximise revenue.[12] Below the agent, **middlemen** — tenants who held large leases and sublet in smaller parcels at a profit — added another layer of extraction. By the time rent obligations reached the actual farmer, they had often been **rack-rented** to the maximum the land could bear and sometimes beyond it. The constant threat was **eviction**. A tenant who fell into arrears could be turned out with little legal recourse. Eviction meant the loss of the cabin, the garden, and any improvements the tenant had made — since under Irish law, improvements belonged to the landlord. The 1820s saw a rising tide of evictions as landlords, themselves squeezed by falling incomes, moved to consolidate holdings and clear out smallholders who could not pay.

## Tithes

If rent was the heaviest burden, **tithes** were the most resented. The **tithe system** required every occupier of land to pay one-tenth of the produce of that land to the **Church of Ireland** — the established Protestant church to which roughly 10% of the population belonged.[4] This meant that Catholic farmers, who had their own priests and chapels to support, were compelled by law to fund a church they did not attend and whose clergy they regarded as instruments of the Ascendancy. The tithe was assessed on tillage crops, which meant it fell disproportionately on the poorest farmers — those growing grain on small plots — while graziers running cattle on large pastures often escaped it entirely.[5] **Tithe proctors**, the men who assessed and collected the payments, were among the most hated figures in the countryside. Resistance to tithes was endemic, ranging from legal disputes to outright violence. The **Tithe War** of the 1830s was still a decade away[6], but the resentment that fuelled it was already deep and bitter in the 1820s.

## Market Towns and Fair Days

The commercial life of rural Ireland revolved around **market towns** and their weekly markets and periodic fairs. For a parish like Kilteevan, the nearest market town was **Roscommon**, the county seat, with its market square, its shops, and its weekly market day where farmers brought grain, butter, eggs, and livestock to sell and bought what they could not make — salt, tobacco, iron goods, cloth. **Strokestown**, to the north, and **Boyle**, further north still, served similar functions for their hinterlands. But the great events of the economic calendar were the **fairs**. Held quarterly or on fixed dates tied to saints' days and ancient custom, fairs were where the serious business happened — the buying and selling of cattle, horses, and sheep on a scale that weekly markets could not accommodate. **Fair days** were also social occasions of enormous importance: a chance to meet people from beyond one's own townland, to hear news, to drink, to settle old scores, and to conduct the informal diplomacy of rural life. **Hiring fairs**, held in spring and autumn, were where labourers and servants offered themselves for employment — standing in the town square to be inspected and bargained over by prospective employers. **Horse fairs** and **cattle fairs** drew dealers from across the province and beyond.

## Cottage Industry Under Pressure

Many farming households supplemented their income through **cottage industry**, particularly **spinning and weaving**. Women and children carded and spun wool or flax in the home, and the cloth was either used domestically or sold to merchants. In parts of Connacht, knitting stockings for sale was a significant source of cash income. But by the 1820s, cottage industry was under severe pressure from **factory competition**. The mechanisation of spinning in English and Scottish mills was destroying the market for hand-spun yarn, and hand-loom weavers were being undercut by power looms. The collapse of domestic textile production removed one of the few non-agricultural sources of income available to the rural poor and deepened their dependence on the land.

## Butter, Linen, and Livestock

Ireland's most important agricultural exports told the story of a colonial economy geared to serving British markets. The **butter trade** was enormous. Irish butter, packed in firkins and graded for quality, was exported in vast quantities through the **Cork Butter Exchange**, which was the largest butter market in the world.[7] The Exchange set grades and prices, and its influence extended throughout Munster and into Connacht. A farmer's wife who made good butter could earn meaningful income, and the butter trade was one of the few areas where women's labour translated directly into cash. The **linen trade** was concentrated in **Ulster**, where the growing of **flax** and the weaving of **linen cloth** had created a rural industrial economy distinct from the rest of Ireland.[8] But flax was grown and linen woven on a smaller scale in other provinces too, and for a Connacht farmer, a field of flax could be a valuable cash crop. The **cattle and livestock trade** was the other great pillar. Ireland exported enormous numbers of live cattle to Britain, and the business of breeding, fattening, and driving cattle to fairs and ports was central to the agricultural economy. **Graziers** — men who rented large tracts of pasture to fatten cattle for the market — were a distinct and sometimes resented class, occupying land that smallholders believed should be tilled for food.

## Currency, Credit, and Smuggling

The monetary system of 1820s Ireland was a patchwork. The **Irish pound**, which had traded at a discount to the British pound since the eighteenth century, was formally abolished in **1826** and merged with sterling.[9] Before that date, transactions could involve Irish pounds, British pounds, or a confusing mixture of both, and in rural areas, **barter** remained common — labour exchanged for potato ground, butter for shop goods, a day's work for a day's meals. **Local tokens** issued by merchants circulated in some areas. For most of the rural poor, cash was scarce and precious, to be hoarded for rent day and spent with extreme reluctance.

Where the official economy failed to provide, the **unofficial economy** stepped in. **Smuggling** was widespread, both coastal and inland. Along the western and southern coasts, boats brought in **tobacco, tea, spirits, and silk** from the Continent, evading customs duties that made these goods unaffordable through legal channels. Inland, the most significant form of smuggling was the production and sale of **poitin** (also spelled *poitín*) — illicit whiskey distilled from grain or potatoes in hidden stills scattered across the countryside.[10] Poitin was ubiquitous in rural Ireland, cheaper and often preferred to legal spirits, and its suppression was a running battle between **revenue police** and communities that regarded distilling as a natural right.

## The Shopkeeper and Publican Class

One of the most significant social developments of the early nineteenth century was the emergence of the **Catholic middle class**, and at its heart stood the **shopkeeper-publican**. In many small towns and villages, the local shop and the local public house were the same establishment, run by the same man, and that man was increasingly a figure of considerable local power. He sold goods on **credit** to farmers who had no cash between harvests, and that credit bound them to him. He bought their produce — eggs, butter, grain — often at prices he set himself. He lent money. He provided a meeting place. He might also serve as a political organiser, a fixer, a go-between with the authorities. The **gombeen man** — the rural money-lender and shopkeeper who profited from the poverty of his neighbours — was already a recognisable figure, though the term itself came later. The relationship between farmer and shopkeeper was one of mutual dependence laced with mutual resentment: the farmer needed credit, the shopkeeper needed customers, and the power imbalance was always present.

## The Scale of Poverty

It is difficult to overstate the **poverty** of the bottom third of Irish rural society in the 1820s. **Labourers and cottiers** — perhaps two million people — lived in one-room cabins of mud and stone, slept on straw, owned almost nothing, and ate potatoes and little else for most of the year. Their clothing was ragged. Their children were often barefoot even in winter. When work was unavailable — and agricultural work was seasonal, leaving long months of enforced idleness — they had no recourse. There was no **Poor Law** in Ireland until **1838**, no workhouses, no system of parish relief as existed in England.[11] Charity came from the Catholic Church, from neighbours almost as poor as themselves, and from begging. Wandering beggars were a constant presence on Irish roads, and the fear of joining their ranks haunted every smallholder one bad harvest away from ruin.

## Game Design Implications

The economic structures of 1820s Ireland offer rich material for gameplay systems and NPC behaviour. Every character in the parish exists within a web of **economic relationships** — tenant to landlord, farmer to shopkeeper, labourer to employer, debtor to creditor — and these relationships should drive dialogue, quests, and social dynamics. A farmer might speak cautiously about the landlord's agent because he fears eviction; a shopkeeper might offer the player credit with strings attached; a labourer might be desperate enough to take risks that a strong farmer would refuse.

**Market and fair days** should function as major game events — periodic gatherings that change the social landscape of the parish, bringing in outside characters, creating opportunities for trade and information-gathering, and generating the kind of concentrated social interaction that fairs provided in reality. The quarterly fair is a natural setting for hiring mechanics, livestock trading, news dissemination, and encounters with characters from beyond the player's usual circle.

**Class tensions** should be palpable throughout the game. The tithe proctor's visit, the agent's rent collection, the grazier's fenced-off pastures — these are not background flavour but active sources of conflict that NPCs should reference, react to, and involve the player in. The simmering resentment toward tithes, the anxiety of rent day, and the desperation of eviction are emotional anchors that ground the player in the period.

**Trade mechanics** might include selling produce at market, managing credit with the local shopkeeper, deciding whether to buy legal goods or cheaper smuggled alternatives, or participating in the poitin economy with its risks and rewards. The dual economy of subsistence and market agriculture means NPCs should behave differently depending on the season and the state of prices — generous after a good harvest, tight-fisted and anxious when the rent is due. Currency confusion in the period before 1826 could even serve as a minor gameplay element, with sharp dealers exploiting the exchange rate between Irish and British pounds.

The fundamental economic reality — that most people were poor, that security was fragile, and that the systems meant to help did not yet exist — should pervade the tone of the game. Parish is not a world of abundance. It is a world where a pig sold at fair might save a family, where credit from the shopkeeper comes at a cost, and where the wrong word to the wrong person can set in motion a chain of consequences that ends at the side of the road with nowhere to go.

## Fact-Check Notes

[1] **Verified.** Irish agriculture boomed during the Napoleonic Wars due to British demand for food. By the 1820s Ireland supplied roughly 75% of Britain's imports of grain, meat, and butter. — Wikipedia, "Economic history of Ireland"; Encyclopedia.com, "Agriculture: 1690 to 1845."

[2] **Verified.** The post-Napoleonic depression (1815-1821) devastated Irish agriculture as wartime demand collapsed and grain prices fell sharply. — Wikipedia, "Post-Napoleonic depression."

[3] **Partially verified.** Wheat prices did fall dramatically after the wars: the price of corn fell from 126s 6d per quarter in 1812 to 65s 7d by 1815. The document's claim that wheat "fetched over 80 shillings a quarter during the war" is technically true but significantly understated — wartime prices peaked well above 100 shillings. The claim that prices fell "to under 40 shillings by the early 1820s" may be somewhat overstated; 80 shillings was the Corn Law threshold price set in 1815, and domestic grain prices generally remained above 40 shillings. — Spartacus Educational, "The Corn Laws"; napoleon-series.org, "Average Price of Wheat Per Quarter, 1800-1814."

[4] **Verified.** The tithe system required all occupiers of land to pay one-tenth of produce to the established Church of Ireland. Catholics, who were the vast majority, were compelled to fund a church they did not attend. — Wikipedia, "Tithe War"; Encyclopedia.com, "Tithe War (1830-1838)."

[5] **Verified, with nuance.** Tithes originally fell disproportionately on tillage farmers. However, after the Tithes Composition Act 1823, legislation increasingly extended tithes to grasslands, which was a factor in swelling opposition. The statement is accurate for the pre-1823 period. — National Archives of Ireland, "The Tithe Applotment Books, 1823-37."

[6] **Verified.** The Tithe War is conventionally dated 1830-1836 (some sources say 1830-1838). It began in autumn 1830 in Graiguenamanagh, Co. Kilkenny, when Father Martin Doyle counselled his parishioners to withhold payment. — Wikipedia, "Tithe War."

[7] **Verified.** The Cork Butter Exchange, formally established in 1769, was the largest butter market in the world for much of the 19th century and set the global price for butter. — The Butter Museum, Cork; Irish Times, "Butter days — An Irishman's Diary."

[8] **Verified.** The Ulster linen industry created a distinct rural industrial economy. By the late 1790s, linen and related goods accounted for 56.5% of Irish exports. An 1825 Select Committee described it as "a mixture of agriculture and manufacture." — Ferguson's Irish Linen; The Cottage Linen Weavers, Ulster American Folk Park.

[9] **Verified.** The Irish pound was merged with sterling in 1826 under the Assimilation of Currencies Act. The conversion rate had long been fixed at 13 Irish pounds to 12 sterling. — Wikipedia, "Irish pound"; History Ireland, "Using Other People's Money."

[10] **Verified.** Poitin (from Irish *pota*, "little pot") was widely distilled from grain, potatoes, or sugar using small copper pot stills. Distilling without a licence had been illegal since 1556 but was ubiquitous in rural areas. — Wikipedia, "Poitin"; RTE Brainstorm, "A short history of Irish Poitin."

[11] **Verified.** The Poor Relief (Ireland) Act 1838 was the first comprehensive poor law for Ireland, establishing the workhouse system. Before 1838, Ireland had no statutory system of poor relief comparable to England's Elizabethan Poor Law, though limited Houses of Industry existed in some areas. — Wikipedia, "Irish poor laws"; The Workhouse, "The 1838 Irish Poor Law Act."

[12] **Verified.** Absentee landlordism, land agents, and the middleman system of subletting at profit were well-documented features of the Irish land system. — Encyclopedia.com, "Agriculture: 1690 to 1845"; Marquette Law Review, "The Land-Tenure System in Ireland."
