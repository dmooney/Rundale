# Food & Drink in 1820s Ireland

*Research document for Rundale — an interactive fiction game set in 1820s rural Ireland.*

## The Potato Economy

By the 1820s, the potato had become the foundation of life for the Irish rural poor to a degree that astonished contemporary visitors. An adult male labourer might consume **ten to fourteen pounds of potatoes per day** [1], a figure that sounds scarcely credible but is well attested in the evidence of the period. The dominant variety was the **"lumper"** [2], a high-yielding but watery and tasteless potato that had displaced earlier, more flavourful varieties precisely because it produced more food per acre on the small plots available to cottiers and labourers. A family of six could subsist on the output of roughly one and a half acres of potatoes, and since even the poorest tenant could usually access at least a potato patch through the **conacre** system — seasonal land rental paid in labour or cash — the tuber sustained a population that had swelled enormously in the decades since 1780. The potato was not merely the main dish; it was breakfast, dinner, and supper, eaten from a **skib** (a shallow wicker basket) [3] placed in the centre of the family, with each person peeling and eating by hand.

This dependence was not born of ignorance but of economic logic. Grain — oats, wheat, barley — was grown extensively across Ireland, but it was a **cash crop** destined for the landlord's rent or the export market. The same acre that could feed a family on potatoes would yield only a fraction of the calories if planted in grain. The potato thus underwrote a system in which Irish soil fed both its own people and a substantial portion of Britain, though the two streams of food moved in very different directions: grain and livestock flowed east to English markets, while the potato stayed in the cabin.

## Buttermilk, Oatmeal, and the Daily Diet

The essential accompaniment to the potato was **buttermilk**, the thin, sour liquid left over after churning butter. Since butter-making was widespread even among the poor (butter being another cash product), buttermilk was readily available and provided the protein, fat, and calcium that potatoes alone lacked. A labourer's daily intake typically consisted of potatoes dipped in or washed down with buttermilk, sometimes with a pinch of salt or a raw onion for variety. In better-off households, potatoes might be mashed with butter or milk to make **champ** (with scallions) or **colcannon** (with cabbage or kale) [4], but for the poorest families, the unadorned combination of boiled lumpers and buttermilk was the unvarying reality.

**Oatmeal** served as a secondary staple, particularly in the northern and western counties. It was most commonly prepared as **stirabout** — oatmeal boiled in water or milk to make a thick porridge — and was a valued food especially during the anxious summer months. **Oatcakes** and oat bread, baked on a griddle over the hearth, supplemented the diet in regions where oats grew well. In parts of Connacht and Ulster, stirabout occupied a more prominent place in the daily meal than it did in the midlands, where the potato reigned virtually unchallenged.

## The Meal Months and Seasonal Hunger

The Irish food calendar had a terrifying structural vulnerability built into it. Each year, the **"meal months"** of July and August brought a period of acute scarcity [5]. The previous year's potato crop would be exhausted or rotting by midsummer, while the new season's potatoes were not yet ready to dig. During this gap, families survived on whatever oatmeal they could afford, on foraged plants such as nettles and wild garlic, on charity, or simply on hunger. The meal months were a time of visible hardship — thinned faces, children begging on roads, families eating seed potatoes meant for the next planting in an act of desperate short-termism. This annual cycle of plenty and want was a defining rhythm of rural life, and it meant that even in years of adequate harvest, the summer brought real suffering to the poorest.

## The Paradox of Pre-Famine Nutrition

Despite the monotony and precariousness of this diet, there is a striking paradox in the nutritional evidence. The combination of **potatoes and buttermilk** provided a remarkably complete nutritional profile: potatoes supplied carbohydrates, vitamin C, potassium, and some B vitamins, while buttermilk contributed protein, fat, and calcium. Studies of pre-Famine skeletal remains and contemporary observations suggest that the Irish rural poor were often **taller and more physically robust** than their English counterparts labouring in the new industrial cities [6], where diets of white bread, weak tea, and adulterated food produced widespread malnutrition. Irish recruits to the British army were frequently noted for their height and strength. The potato diet was monotonous, vulnerable, and culturally despised by outside observers, but it was not nutritionally deficient — a cruel irony given what the Famine would soon reveal about its fragility.

## Meat, Fish, and Protein

Meat was a rarity on the tables of the poor. **Cattle** were raised across Ireland in enormous numbers, but they were an export commodity and a source of rent money, not food for the people who tended them. A labouring family might taste meat a handful of times a year: at **Christmas**, when a piece of salt beef or bacon might appear; at a **fair day**, when a penny could buy a bit of offal; or at a **wake**, where the dead person's family was honour-bound to provide generously. When meat was available, it was most often **pork or bacon**, since a pig could be fattened on scraps and potato peelings and was the one animal the poor could realistically keep and slaughter. The pig was famously called "the gentleman that pays the rent" [7], though in practice one might be kept back for the household's own use.

Along the coast, **fish and seaweed** supplemented the diet significantly. Herring, mackerel, and shellfish were seasonal resources, and various seaweeds — particularly **dulse** and **carrageen moss** [8] — were gathered, dried, and eaten or used to thicken dishes. Inland communities had less access to these coastal foods, which contributed to meaningful **regional variation** in diet and nutrition.

## Drink: Poitín, Ale, Whiskey, and Tea

Alcohol occupied a central place in Irish social life, and the most distinctive drink of the countryside was **poitín** (anglicised as "poteen"), an illicit spirit distilled from grain, potatoes, or sugar in small copper stills hidden in bogs, barns, and mountainsides. Poitín production was a **massive cottage industry** in the 1820s, pursued vigorously by **revenue officers** (the "gaugers") but deeply embedded in rural culture. The quality varied wildly — a skilled distiller produced a clean, potent spirit, while careless work could yield something genuinely dangerous. Poitín featured at every communal gathering: wakes, weddings, christenings, fair days, and work parties such as the **meitheal** (communal harvest labour). Offering poitín was a gesture of hospitality and trust, and refusing it could give offence.

Legal **whiskey** and **ale** were available through licensed premises, but in rural areas the more important institution was the **shebeen** — an unlicensed drinking house, often simply a cabin where a woman brewed ale or a man sold poitín. Shebeens served as informal social clubs, places of news and gossip, matchmaking venues, and occasionally sites of faction fighting. The distinction between a licensed public house and a shebeen was significant in law but often blurred in practice, particularly in remote townlands far from magistrates.

**Tea** was just beginning its long penetration of Irish rural life in the 1820s. It remained largely a **luxury and status marker** [13], associated with the gentry and the more prosperous strong farmers. A woman who served tea to visitors was making a statement about her household's standing. By mid-century tea would become universal, but in the 1820s it was still a novelty in most cabins, and offering it carried social meaning that offering buttermilk did not.

## Cooking and the Hearth

The centre of every cabin was the **open hearth**, where a turf fire burned more or less continuously. Cooking was done over this fire using a small number of essential implements. The **three-legged iron pot** was the primary vessel, used to boil potatoes in their skins — the universal daily act of food preparation. The **bastible** (or **bastable**) [11], a heavy cast-iron pot with a flat lid on which turf embers could be heaped, served as an oven for baking soda bread and cakes. The **griddle**, a flat iron plate, was used for oatcakes and later for **boxty** (potato pancakes) [12]. These three items — pot, bastible, and griddle — represented a significant investment for a poor family and were among their most valued possessions. Cooking was women's work, and skill at the hearth was a valued quality in a wife, particularly the ability to manage the heat of a turf fire, which burned cooler and less evenly than wood or coal.

## Feast Foods and Celebration

Communal celebrations revealed a richer culinary tradition than daily life might suggest. At **wakes**, the family of the deceased provided lavishly: tobacco, snuff, candles, whiskey or poitín, and food that might include bread, butter, cold meat, and sometimes a whole meal. The wake was both a solemn obligation and a social event, and the quality of its provisions reflected on the family's honour. **Weddings** called for similar generosity, with a wedding feast that might feature **colcannon**, **boxty**, a goose or hen, bacon, and abundant drink. **Christmas** was the great food holiday: families who ate potatoes and buttermilk for 364 days would scrape together the means for a piece of **spiced beef or bacon**, a goose if they were fortunate, and perhaps a cake or pudding. These feast occasions served as markers in the calendar, events that people anticipated, saved for, and remembered.

## The Butter Trade and Class

The **Cork Butter Exchange**, established in 1770 [9], was by the 1820s the **largest butter market in the world**. Butter produced across Munster and beyond was packed into wooden **firkins** [10], graded, weighed, and exported in enormous quantities to Britain, the Caribbean, and continental Europe. This trade meant that the butter churned in a Roscommon cabin was a commodity with an international price, and the economic logic was clear: sell the butter, keep the buttermilk. The same principle applied across the food system. The poor produced food of considerable quality and variety — butter, grain, livestock, eggs — but consumed almost none of it themselves. Diet was thus a stark **class marker**: a strong farmer ate bread, butter, meat, and eggs; a cottier ate potatoes and buttermilk. The merchant and gentry classes ate food that would be recognisable on an English table. Observing what a person ate, drank, and served to visitors told you almost everything about their place in the social hierarchy.

## Game Design Implications

The food economy of 1820s Ireland offers rich material for Rundale's simulation of daily life. **NPC daily routines** should revolve around food preparation and acquisition: women tending the fire and boiling the evening potatoes, men working fields that grow crops they will never eat, children sent to collect buttermilk or forage for nettles during the meal months. The **seasonal cycle** is critical — the game clock should register the difference between autumn plenty (new potatoes, harvest surplus) and the anxious hunger of July and August, when NPC dialogue and behaviour shift toward scarcity and worry.

**Pubs and shebeens** function as the primary social hubs of the game world. They are where the player will overhear gossip, encounter strangers, witness disputes, and gather information. The distinction between a respectable licensed house and a rough shebeen provides natural social geography: different characters frequent different establishments, and the player's choice of drinking venue signals their social affiliations. Poitín distillation can serve as a recurring background activity — a neighbour's still, a revenue officer's raid, the tension between community solidarity and the law.

**Food as a class marker** should be visible throughout the game. When the player enters a strong farmer's house, they should see bread, butter, and perhaps tea on the table; in a labourer's cabin, a skib of potatoes and a jug of buttermilk. What an NPC offers the player to eat or drink communicates social standing and the degree of welcome being extended. Feast events — wakes, weddings, Christmas, fair days — provide natural set-piece moments where the community gathers, social rules relax, drink flows, and narrative possibilities multiply. These gatherings are where secrets slip out, alliances form, and grudges surface, making them ideal nodes in the game's story structure.

## Fact-Check Notes

[1] **Verified.** Multiple sources confirm that by the beginning of the nineteenth century, men labouring in the fields consumed ten to fourteen pounds of potatoes a day, washed down with milk or buttermilk. — Britannica, "Great Famine"; RTÉ, "Why was the potato so important?"

[2] **Verified.** The Lumper outyielded other varieties by as much as 30 percent and spread rapidly due to its higher yields and adaptability to poor soils. It earned the reputation of being "wet, tasteless and unwholesome," though modern tasting tests have found it comparable to ordinary potatoes. — History Ireland, "The Lumper Potato and the Famine"; National Geographic, "Meet the Lumper."

[3] **Verified.** The skib is a traditional Irish potato basket made from hand-woven willow, used to strain and serve potatoes. It was placed at the centre of the gathering as a communal serving vessel. — Hanna Van Aelst, "Traditional Irish Potato Basket: The Skib."

[4] **Verified.** Champ is mashed potatoes with chopped scallions or spring onions. Colcannon is mashed potatoes with cabbage (or sometimes kale), often with additional ingredients like leeks or scallions. Both are well-documented traditional Irish dishes. — Multiple culinary sources.

[5] **Verified.** During the "meal months" of July and August (particularly July into early August), old potato stocks were depleted or unfit for consumption. The first potato harvest beginning around 1 August marked the end of this period of privation. — RTÉ Brainstorm, "The 'hungry gap' in 19th century Ireland."

[6] **Verified.** Research titled "Tall But Poor: Nutrition, Health, and Living Standards in Pre-Famine Ireland" confirms that the Irish "attained roughly the 16th centile of modern height standards and, though smaller than contemporary North Americans, were among the tallest in Europe, including the wealthier English." — NBER Working Paper, "Tall But Poor."

[7] **Verified.** The pig would be sold on "gale day" (twice-yearly rent collection dates) to pay rent, hence the euphemism "the gentleman who pays the rent." — Irish American Mom, "The Irish Pig."

[8] **Verified.** Both dulse (dillisk) and carrageen moss (Irish moss) are confirmed as edible seaweeds harvested in Ireland for centuries. Carrageen moss has been gathered using rakes designed to preserve regeneration. — seaweed.ie, "Use of seaweed as food in Ireland."

[9] **Verified.** The Cork Butter Exchange opened in 1770 and was "the largest butter market in the world" for much of the 19th century. By the 1820s, the operation involved fourteen export merchants, seven butter buyers, and three tanners. — Irish Times, "Butter days"; The Butter Museum, Cork.

[10] **Verified.** A firkin is a small wooden barrel (made from oak, sycamore, or other hardwoods) used to ship and store butter. Firkins were soaked in salt water before use and often sealed with salt water to prevent spoiling. — CooksInfo, "Firkins."

[11] **Verified.** A bastible is a flat-bottomed, cast-iron pot with three legs for stability, a hinged or removable flat lid designed to hold hot sods of turf for even top heating, and a handle for suspension over the fire. — Irish American Mom, "What Is A Bastible?"

[12] **Verified.** Boxty is a traditional Irish potato pancake made from grated and mashed potatoes, fried until crispy, dating to the 1700s. The dish is particularly associated with northern Irish regions (Leitrim, Mayo, Sligo, Fermanagh, Longford, Cavan). — Multiple culinary sources.

[13] **Verified.** Tea arrived in Ireland in the early 1800s as a luxury for the wealthy upper class. While becoming more affordable by the early 19th century, it remained far from universal in rural areas during the 1820s. Middle and upper-class critics viewed peasant tea drinking as inappropriate class elevation. — Irish Central, "A timeline of tea in Ireland."
