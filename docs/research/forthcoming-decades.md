# The Forthcoming Decades — What's Coming After 1820

> **Design note:** This document covers events *after* the game's starting period of the early 1820s. NPCs living in Kilteevan, County Roscommon, do not know these things are coming. This material exists for game designers to inform foreshadowing, dramatic irony, and long-term narrative planning should the game timeline advance. The characters go about their daily lives unaware that they stand at the edge of a precipice.

## A Hinge Point in Irish History

The 1820s occupy a deceptive position in Irish history. On the surface, the rhythms of rural life in a parish like Kilteevan appear timeless — the same potato ridges, the same rent days, the same patterns of fair and market, Mass and holy well. But the structural conditions for catastrophe are already firmly in place. **Potato dependency** among the cottier and labourer classes is near-total. **Population growth** is pressing hard against the carrying capacity of subdivided holdings. The **landlord system** extracts rent from tenants who have no security of tenure and no legal standing to improve their own land. The **Penal Law** legacy, though partially dismantled, still excludes Catholics from political power. Every one of these conditions will, within a single generation, feed into disasters that will empty townlands across Roscommon and transform Ireland beyond recognition.

## Catholic Emancipation and Its Bitter Bargain (1829)

The most immediate political transformation comes with **Catholic Emancipation** in 1829. **Daniel O'Connell**, already a towering figure in the 1820s through his work with the **Catholic Association** and the penny-a-month **Catholic Rent**, wins a spectacular victory when he is elected MP for Clare in 1828 despite being legally barred from taking his seat. The British government, fearing mass unrest, passes the **Roman Catholic Relief Act** in 1829, permitting Catholics to sit in Parliament and hold most public offices for the first time since the seventeenth century. For communities like Kilteevan, this feels like the dawn of a new era — the great champion has broken the Protestant monopoly on power. But the victory carries a sting. The **40-shilling freehold franchise**, which had allowed even modest Catholic tenant farmers to vote, is simultaneously raised to **£10**, disenfranchising the vast majority of the very people who carried O'Connell to his triumph. The Catholic middle class gains access to power; the Catholic poor lose what little political voice they had. This pattern — reform that benefits the few while abandoning the many — will recur throughout the coming decades.

## The Tithe War (1830s)

Hard on the heels of Emancipation comes the **Tithe War**, an eruption of organized resistance in the early 1830s against the obligation of all Irish people, regardless of faith, to pay **tithes** supporting the established **Church of Ireland**. For Catholic and Presbyterian communities who derive no spiritual benefit from the Established Church, this extraction is a source of deep and justified resentment. The resistance takes forms both peaceful and violent — mass refusal to pay, the driving off of tithe proctors, and occasional bloody confrontations such as the **Battle of Carrickshock** in 1831. The government responds with coercion, and tithe collection is sometimes enforced at bayonet point, with cattle and goods seized from defaulters. The conflict simmers throughout the decade until the **Tithe Commutation Act of 1838** converts the tithe into a rent charge paid by landlords rather than tenants directly — a compromise that reduces the visibility of the extraction without eliminating it. For a parish like Kilteevan, where the Church of Ireland rector may serve a tiny Protestant congregation while the Catholic majority pays for his upkeep, the tithe issue is already a live grievance in the 1820s, and its escalation into open warfare lies just around the corner.

## National Schools and the Decline of Irish (1831)

In 1831, Chief Secretary **Edward Stanley** issues the letter that establishes the **National School system** in Ireland, creating a network of state-funded primary schools intended to provide non-denominational education. The system is a remarkable success in one regard — **literacy rates** climb dramatically over the following decades, and Ireland becomes one of the most literate societies in Europe. But the schools teach exclusively through **English**, and the effect on the **Irish language** is devastating. Parents who see English as the language of advancement and emigration increasingly encourage their children to abandon Irish. The hedge schools that have sustained Irish-language learning in the penal era are gradually supplanted. In a parish like Kilteevan in the 1820s, Irish is still the everyday language of most of the population, though English is gaining ground among the ambitious and the mobile. Within a generation, the balance will tip irreversibly. The national schools accelerate a linguistic shift that was already underway, but they do so with the force of institutional authority and the promise of social mobility.

## The Poor Law and the Workhouse (1838)

The **Irish Poor Law of 1838**, modelled on the English system, divides Ireland into **130 Poor Law Unions**, each centred on a market town with a **workhouse** built to house the destitute. The workhouse system is designed around the principle of **"less eligibility"** — conditions inside must be worse than the worst conditions outside, to deter all but the truly desperate from seeking relief. Families are separated on entry, men from women, parents from children. The diet is minimal, the regime harsh, the stigma profound. Roscommon town gets its workhouse, and it will become one of the most dreaded institutions in the county. In the 1820s, the destitute rely on informal charity, the goodwill of neighbours, and begging — a precarious existence, but one that at least preserves family and dignity. The workhouse represents something new and terrible: the institutionalisation of poverty, the reduction of human beings to paupers managed by the state. When the Famine arrives, the workhouses will be overwhelmed beyond any capacity their designers imagined.

## The Repeal Movement and the Monster Meetings (1840s)

Flushed with the triumph of Emancipation, **O'Connell** turns his attention to a larger prize: the **Repeal of the Act of Union** that bound Ireland to Britain in 1800. Through the 1840s, the **Repeal Association** organises massive open-air gatherings known as **"monster meetings"**, drawing crowds estimated in the hundreds of thousands to sites of historical and symbolic significance across Ireland. The movement reaches its crescendo in 1843, when O'Connell plans a climactic meeting at **Clontarf**, site of Brian Boru's legendary victory. The British government bans the meeting at the last moment, and O'Connell, committed to constitutional methods and terrified of bloodshed, complies. The cancellation is a turning point — it discredits O'Connell in the eyes of younger, more radical nationalists and fractures the movement. Within two years, the Famine will render the question of Repeal almost irrelevant, swallowed by the sheer scale of human suffering.

## The Great Famine (1845–1852)

Everything in this document leads toward and is dwarfed by the **Great Famine**, the defining catastrophe of nineteenth-century Ireland. In the autumn of 1845, a previously unknown pathogen — **Phytophthora infestans**, the potato blight — arrives in Ireland, turning potato crops to black, putrid mush in the fields. The blight strikes again in 1846, and repeatedly thereafter. For the millions of Irish people who depend on the potato as their primary or sole food source, the result is **mass starvation** on a scale unprecedented in modern European history. **Typhus**, **relapsing fever**, and **dysentery** ravage weakened populations. The **workhouses** are overwhelmed, turning away the dying. The government establishes **soup kitchens** and **public works schemes**, but the response is widely regarded as catastrophically inadequate, shaped by laissez-faire ideology and an indifference to Irish suffering that many then and since have characterised as callous or genocidal. **County Roscommon** is among the hardest-hit regions. Townlands are emptied. Entire families perish or are scattered. Ireland's population drops from approximately **8.2 million** to roughly **6.5 million** through death and emigration combined, and the decline will continue for decades after the blight recedes.

The Famine is not a natural disaster alone — it is a catastrophe shaped by political and economic structures. The **potato dependency** visible in Kilteevan in the 1820s, the **subdivision of holdings** into plots too small to support a family by any other means, the **export of grain and livestock** from Ireland even as people starve, the **landlord system** that treats tenants as disposable — all of these are present and observable in the game's timeframe. The Famine does not come from nowhere. It comes from the world the player can already see.

## Emigration and the Diaspora

The Famine unleashes a wave of **mass emigration** that transforms not only Ireland but the receiving nations. **Coffin ships** — overcrowded, disease-ridden vessels — carry the desperate to **America**, **Canada**, **Australia**, and **England**. Mortality rates on some crossings approach those of slave ships. Those who survive carry with them memories of hunger, dispossession, and what they perceive as British betrayal, fuelling a diasporic nationalism that will shape Irish politics for generations. Even before the Famine, emigration is a feature of Irish life in the 1820s — seasonal migration to England for harvest work, permanent departures by those with the means to leave. But the Famine turns a trickle into a torrent. By the century's end, more Irish people will live outside Ireland than within it.

## Revolutionary Nationalism: Young Irelanders and Fenians

The collapse of O'Connell's constitutional movement and the trauma of the Famine give rise to a more radical strand of nationalism. The **Young Irelanders** of 1848, inspired by revolutionary movements across Europe, attempt an armed rising that fails almost farcically — the **Battle of Ballingarry** amounts to a skirmish in a cabbage garden. But the ideas planted do not die. The next generation produces the **Fenian Brotherhood** (the **Irish Republican Brotherhood**) in the 1860s, a secret oath-bound organisation dedicated to achieving Irish independence by force. The Fenians too will fail in their immediate aims, but they establish a revolutionary tradition that leads, through many turns, to 1916 and beyond. In the 1820s, the secret societies active in places like Kilteevan — **Ribbonmen**, **Rockites**, local agrarian combinations — are precursors of this tradition, operating with the same methods of oath, secrecy, and nocturnal action, though their aims are local rather than national.

## The Long Struggle for the Land

The question that dominates daily life in Kilteevan — who owns the land and on what terms — will not be resolved for decades. The **Land War** of **1879–1882**, organised by the **Irish National Land League** under **Michael Davitt** and **Charles Stewart Parnell**, finally brings the landlord system under sustained, organised assault through rent strikes, boycotts (the very word derives from Captain Boycott, a land agent in County Mayo), and political agitation. A succession of **Land Acts** from 1870 onward gradually transfers ownership from landlords to tenants, a process not completed until the early twentieth century. The world the player inhabits in the 1820s — a world where a landlord's agent can evict a family on a whim, where tenants build houses and clear fields they can never own, where rent is the axis around which all life turns — will eventually be dismantled. But not before the Famine has done its worst.

## Game Design Implications

This timeline is not background lore — it is a design resource with specific applications for Parish.

**Foreshadowing through NPC dialogue** is the most immediate use. Characters in the 1820s cannot predict the Famine, but they can voice the anxieties that make it inevitable. A farmer noting that his holding is too small to divide again among his sons. A mother observing that the family eats nothing but potatoes from August to May. An old man remembering the partial failure of 1816–1817 and wondering what would happen if the crop failed entirely. A priest worrying about the growing population and the shrinking holdings. None of these characters know what is coming, but the player — armed with historical knowledge — can recognise the warning signs. This creates **dramatic irony** of extraordinary power: the player knows the catastrophe is approaching and cannot prevent it, lending even ordinary moments a weight of impending loss.

**Timeline advancement**, if the game supports it, transforms the parish utterly. Characters the player knows and cares about will face starvation, emigration, the workhouse, death. The cosy familiarity of the parish becomes a source of dread. The game can use the passage of time as its most powerful narrative mechanism — not through dramatic set-pieces but through absence, through the empty houses and the names no longer spoken.

**The weight of knowing** is perhaps the most distinctive quality this material gives the game. Parish is set in a world that feels stable, rooted, ancient in its rhythms. The research in this document reveals that world to be balanced on a knife's edge. Every potato ridge is a countdown. Every new baby in a subdivided holding is a step closer to catastrophe. The game does not need to depict the Famine to be haunted by it. It only needs to depict, with honesty and care, the world that made the Famine possible — and trust the player to feel the shadow falling.
