# Medicine & Health in 1820s Ireland

## Folk Medicine: The Primary Healthcare of the Rural Poor

In the townlands around Kilteevan, County Roscommon, in the 1820s, the word "doctor" meant something very different from what it would come to mean by the century's end. For the vast majority of the rural Irish poor, formal medicine was a distant, expensive, and largely irrelevant institution. Their healthcare system was older, rooted in generations of accumulated knowledge passed down within families and communities. **Folk medicine** was not a supplement to professional care — it was the whole of it. Every household kept some store of herbal knowledge: which plants drew out infection, which bark eased a fever, which roots could settle a troubled stomach. This was practical wisdom, tested by repetition over centuries, and it formed the first and often the only line of defence against illness and injury in a world where the nearest qualified physician might be a full day's journey away and utterly unaffordable even if he could be reached.

## The Bean Feasa and Fear Feasa

At the centre of this folk medical tradition stood the **bean feasa** (wise woman) and the **fear feasa** (wise man) [1] — local healers whose knowledge and reputation set them apart from ordinary people. These were not merely herbalists, though herb lore formed the practical backbone of their craft. The bean feasa combined her remedies with **prayers, charms, and ritual**, drawing on a tradition that blended pre-Christian practice with Catholic devotion in ways that would have scandalised a theologian but seemed perfectly natural to her neighbours. She might recite a specific prayer over a poultice, or insist that a remedy be gathered at a particular phase of the moon, or prescribe a cure that involved visiting a holy well at dawn. To dismiss the ritual elements as superstition is to misunderstand the system entirely: in a world where the boundaries between the physical and spiritual were porous, healing was understood to operate on both planes simultaneously. The bean feasa's authority derived not from any formal credential but from demonstrated results and community trust, and she was consulted on everything from colicky infants to wasting illnesses to problems that a modern observer might classify as psychological rather than physical.

## Bone-Setters

Alongside the general healers, certain families maintained highly specialised reputations as **bone-setters**. The skill of reducing fractures and dislocations was understood to be **hereditary** — passed from parent to child within specific lineages, often for many generations. [3] A bone-setter might be a farmer or labourer in every other respect, but when someone suffered a broken limb or a dislocated shoulder, it was to this person that they were carried, sometimes over considerable distances. Bone-setters were held in extraordinary regard, and their results were often genuinely impressive: they had developed, through long empirical practice, techniques for manipulating bones back into alignment that compared favourably with what formal surgeons of the period could achieve. The bone-setter's art would persist in rural Ireland well into the twentieth century, long after professional medicine had established itself, a testament to the depth of skill these practitioners commanded.

## Midwifery and the Perils of Childbirth

Virtually every birth in 1820s Kilteevan was attended by the **bean ghlúine** — the local midwife, whose title literally means "knee-woman," referring to the kneeling position she assumed during delivery. [2] The bean ghlúine was one of the most important figures in community life, present at the most dangerous and consequential moment a family could face. Childbirth was perilous. **Maternal mortality** claimed women with grim regularity — from haemorrhage, from infection, from obstructed labour that no amount of skill could resolve without surgical intervention that simply did not exist in a rural townland. **Infant mortality** was staggering by modern standards: perhaps **twenty to thirty percent of children died before reaching the age of five** [4], carried off by fevers, dysentery, whooping cough, or simple failure to thrive. The bean ghlúine did what she could with experience, herbal preparations, and prayer. She knew how to turn a breech baby, how to manage the cord, how to staunch bleeding with pressure and specific herbs. But she could not perform a caesarean section, could not treat puerperal fever with antibiotics that would not exist for another century, and so she presided over tragedies as well as joys with a stoic frequency that marked her deeply.

## Common Remedies

The pharmacopoeia of rural Roscommon was the hedgerow and the kitchen garden. **Dock leaves** soothed stings and skin irritations. **Comfrey** — known as "knitbone" — was applied as a poultice to sprains and fractures, and modern science has vindicated its anti-inflammatory properties. [5] **Nettle tea** served as a tonic and blood purifier, rich in iron, and was given to women after childbirth and to anyone suffering from general debility. **Elderflower** was infused for colds and fevers. **Garlic** was used both internally and as a poultice, valued for its ability to fight infection long before anyone understood the mechanism. **Whiskey and poitín** served as anaesthetic, antiseptic, and general-purpose medicine — a tot of poitín for a toothache, a hot whiskey with honey for a chest cold, raw spirits poured into a wound to clean it. Poitín in particular, being locally distilled and untaxed, was the most readily available strong alcohol and doubled as currency, social lubricant, and medical supply in roughly equal measure.

## Holy Wells

Scattered across the landscape were **holy wells** — springs and natural water sources associated with particular saints and believed to possess curative powers. This was not a vague or general belief but a highly specific system: **particular wells were associated with particular ailments** [6]. One well might be visited for **eye complaints**, another for **toothache**, another for **fertility**, another for ailments of the skin. The cure typically involved a ritual circuit of the well — walking around it a prescribed number of times while reciting specific prayers — followed by drinking or bathing in the water and leaving an offering, often a strip of cloth tied to a nearby tree or bush. These **rag trees**, festooned with scraps of fabric, were visible markers of communal faith in the well's power. Holy well visits were especially common on the saint's **pattern day** (feast day), when large gatherings combined devotion, healing, and social occasion. The Catholic clergy had an uneasy relationship with holy wells — the practice was too deeply rooted to suppress but carried overtones of pre-Christian tradition that made bishops uncomfortable.

## Disease and Its Companions

The diseases that stalked 1820s Roscommon were the diseases of poverty. **Typhus** — later called "famine fever" during the Great Famine — was already endemic, spread by lice in overcrowded and unsanitary living conditions. **Dysentery**, both bacillary and amoebic, was a constant threat wherever water sources were contaminated by human and animal waste, which was nearly everywhere. **Tuberculosis** — "consumption" — was widespread and essentially untreatable, a slow death sentence that hollowed out families. **Smallpox** still ravaged communities, though **Jenner's vaccine**, developed in 1796 [7], was beginning its slow spread through Ireland; by the 1820s, some vaccination was occurring, but coverage was deeply uneven, and rural Roscommon was far from the leading edge of adoption. **Measles** and **whooping cough** killed children with particular efficiency, turning common childhood illnesses into lethal events in populations weakened by poor nutrition and chronic exposure to damp, cold housing. The relationship between **poverty, overcrowding, poor sanitation, and disease** was a vicious circle that no amount of herbal skill could break: families of eight or ten lived in single-room cabins with earthen floors, shared their space with livestock, drew water from sources downstream of their waste, and ate a diet that, while calorie-sufficient in good years thanks to the potato, lacked the variety needed to build robust immune function. **Life expectancy** hovered around **thirty-five to forty years** [8], a figure dragged down dramatically by child mortality — those who survived to adulthood could expect to live considerably longer, but the average was brutal.

## The Dispensary System: Formal Medicine Arrives

The 1820s saw the earliest stirrings of the **dispensary system** that would eventually bring some measure of formal medical care to rural Ireland. Funded by **Grand Jury levies** — essentially a local tax administered by the county's Protestant landowning class — dispensaries were intended to provide basic medical care to the poor. [9] A qualified physician or apothecary would be appointed to a dispensary district and was expected to see patients, dispense medicines, and attend emergencies. In practice, the system in the 1820s was **sparse and uneven**: dispensaries were few, underfunded, and often located in towns rather than the remote townlands where need was greatest. The dispensary doctor was typically a Protestant and an outsider, culturally distant from his Catholic patients, and his medicines — calomel, laudanum, bleeding, purging — were no more effective and often considerably more dangerous than the herbs of the bean feasa. **Quack doctors** and **travelling medicine sellers** filled the gaps, moving from fair to fair with bottles of coloured water and bold promises, exploiting desperation with theatrical confidence. The rural poor were not fools — they could distinguish between a remedy that worked and one that did not — but desperation and hope are powerful forces, and a man watching his child sicken will try anything.

## Mental Illness and the Fairies

**Mental illness** occupied a particularly fraught place in 1820s Irish rural life. There was no institutional care — the first public asylums in Ireland were only just being established, and none served a place like Kilteevan. [10] Conditions that a modern clinician might diagnose as schizophrenia, severe depression, bipolar disorder, or the cognitive effects of untreated epilepsy were understood through a framework that blended fear, compassion, and supernatural belief. A person who behaved strangely, who spoke to unseen presences, who withdrew from normal life, might be said to be **"away with the fairies"** — taken or touched by the **sídhe**, the fairy folk of Irish tradition. This was not a metaphor but a genuine explanatory framework: the person's true self had been taken into the fairy fort, and what remained was either a shell or a **changeling**. This belief could lead to both compassion — the afflicted person was a victim, not a sinner — and to terrible interventions intended to drive out the fairy influence or force the fairies to return the real person. The bean feasa might be consulted for cures, and holy wells were visited, but for the most severely affected, there was little anyone could do. They remained within their families, cared for or neglected according to circumstance, invisible to any system of institutional support.

## Folk Medicine versus "Scientific" Medicine

The fundamental reality of healthcare in 1820s Kilteevan was this: **the herb woman was more trusted and more accessible than any doctor**. She spoke the same language — Irish, not English. She lived in the same townland, not in a distant town. She charged little or nothing, or accepted payment in kind — a dozen eggs, a day's labour, a jug of milk. She understood her patients' lives because she shared them. Her remedies, if not always effective, were rarely harmful, which was more than could be said for the heroic medicine of the period with its mercury compounds, its bloodletting, and its blistering plasters. The contrast between folk and formal medicine in this era was not a contrast between ignorance and knowledge but between two incomplete systems, one embedded in the community and the other imposed from outside. For the people of Kilteevan, the choice was obvious, and it was not until formal medicine actually began to produce better outcomes — a development still decades away — that the balance would begin to shift.

## Game Design Implications

The medical landscape of 1820s Kilteevan offers rich material for Parish. A **bean feasa NPC** should be a significant community figure — not a marginal eccentric but a trusted authority consulted by nearly everyone. Her home should be a location players can visit, and her knowledge of herbs, charms, and community secrets should make her a valuable source of both healing and information. A **bone-setter NPC** from a renowned local family adds another specialist role. The **bean ghlúine** (midwife) creates natural narrative tension around births, which were community events fraught with both joy and danger.

**Holy wells** should function as interactive locations with specific associated cures, requiring the player to learn which well addresses which ailment and to perform the correct ritual circuit. Pattern days at holy wells create calendar-driven social events where many NPCs gather. **Disease should operate as a world-level system**: typhus outbreaks, seasonal waves of dysentery, childhood illnesses sweeping through families — these events reshape NPC availability, mood, and behaviour. **Health as an NPC attribute** allows sickness to drive narrative: a sick child creates urgency, a consumptive young person generates pathos, an outbreak strains community resources and tests relationships.

The **dispensary doctor**, if present in the parish, represents the tension between old and new, local and institutional, Irish-speaking and English-speaking worlds. His interactions with the bean feasa — rivalry, grudging respect, mutual incomprehension — mirror larger cultural conflicts. **Travelling quack doctors** arriving at fairs provide colourful one-off encounters. Mental illness and fairy belief create opportunities for morally complex storylines where the player must navigate between supernatural explanation and human suffering, between community tradition and individual welfare.

## Fact-Check Notes

[1] **Verified.** *Bean feasa* (wise woman) and *fear feasa* (wise man) as terms for Irish folk healers who combined herbal medicine with prayers, charms, and ritual. — Confirmed by multiple scholarly and folklore sources, including research published in *Folklore* (Vol. 116, No. 1) via Taylor & Francis, and documented extensively by the Irish Folklore Commission and RTÉ Brainstorm.

[2] **Verified.** *Bean ghlúine* meaning "knee-woman" as a term for midwife. — Confirmed by Wiktionary (citing Ó Dónaill's *Foclóir Gaeilge–Béarla*, 1977) and de Bhaldraithe's *English-Irish Dictionary* (1959). The name likely refers to the kneeling birthing position.

[3] **Verified.** Bone-setting as a hereditary skill passed within specific families in Ireland. — Confirmed by Kingston, R., "A Tale of Two Bone-setters," *Béascna* 8 (2013): 89–102. The concept of an inherited "gift" is well documented, with families practising across multiple generations.

[4] **Unverified.** 20–30% child mortality before age five in 1820s Ireland. — No Ireland-specific data exists for this period, as civil registration did not begin until 1864. The figure is consistent with general estimates for pre-industrial Western European societies (30–50% under-five mortality), but the lower bound of 20% may understate the reality for the rural Irish poor.

[5] **Verified.** Comfrey known as "knitbone," used as a poultice, with scientifically confirmed anti-inflammatory properties. — Confirmed by a clinical overview in *Molecules* (PMC3491633) and *The Pharmaceutical Journal*. Allantoin and rosmarinic acid are the key active compounds; multiple randomized controlled trials support its topical anti-inflammatory efficacy.

[6] **Verified.** Holy wells associated with specific ailments, with ritual circuits and rag trees. — Confirmed by multiple sources including the Irish Post, Emerald Isle heritage resources, and Wikipedia's article on clootie wells. Ireland has over 3,000 documented holy wells, each associated with particular cures (eyes, skin, fertility, etc.), with pattern days (*pátrún*) as annual gatherings.

[7] **Verified.** Jenner's smallpox vaccine developed in 1796. — Confirmed by WHO, CDC, and PMC (Edward Jenner and the History of Smallpox and Vaccination). Jenner inoculated James Phipps on 14 May 1796 using cowpox matter from dairymaid Sarah Nelms.

[8] **Verified.** Life expectancy around 35–40 years in early 19th-century Ireland. — Supported by Statista and the *Economic and Social Review* ("Life Expectancy in Ireland since the 1870s"), which places life expectancy at birth at just over 38 years in the early 1840s. Conditions in the 1820s were likely similar, making 35–40 a reasonable range.

[9] **Verified.** Dispensary system funded by Grand Jury levies. — Confirmed by *The Irish Story* and *History Ireland*. The 1805 Act authorised county grand juries to match voluntary subscriptions for dispensaries. By the early 1830s there were approximately 450 dispensaries, but distribution was uneven, with poorer and more remote areas underserved.

[10] **Verified.** First public asylums in Ireland being established around the 1820s. — Confirmed by NCBI (*The Cost of Insanity in Nineteenth-Century Ireland*). Acts of 1817 and 1821 provided for district asylums for the lunatic poor. The Richmond Asylum opened in 1814; between the 1820s and 1860s, twenty-one asylums were built. The Connacht District Lunatic Asylum at Ballinasloe did not open until 1833.
