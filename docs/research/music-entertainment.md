# Music & Entertainment in 1820s Ireland

## Research Document for Parish (Kilteevan, County Roscommon)

### Instruments of the People

The soundscape of 1820s rural Ireland was shaped by a handful of instruments, each carrying its own social meaning and practical reality. The **fiddle** was far and away the most common instrument in the countryside. It was relatively cheap to acquire, easy to carry from house to house, and versatile enough to handle the full range of Irish dance music — jigs, reels, hornpipes, and slow airs. A parish like Kilteevan would almost certainly have had at least one or two fiddlers of local renown, men (and occasionally women) whose presence at a gathering could transform an ordinary evening into an event. The fiddle required no great infrastructure, no bellows or reeds — just gut strings, a bow, and skill passed from player to player by ear.

The **uilleann pipes** occupied a very different position. This was an instrument of extraordinary complexity, bellows-blown rather than mouth-blown, played while seated, capable of producing a rich, reedy tone across two full chromatic octaves along with drones and regulators that allowed the piper to accompany himself harmonically. The uilleann pipes were distinctly Irish, having evolved across the eighteenth century into something with no real equivalent elsewhere in Europe. A skilled piper was a figure of considerable prestige, but the instrument's complexity and expense meant pipers were rarer than fiddlers. The pipes required constant maintenance — reeds cracking, bags leaking, regulators falling out of tune — and the craft of making and repairing them was itself a specialized art. In County Roscommon in the 1820s, a visiting piper would have been a genuine occasion, someone whose arrival warranted clearing the kitchen floor and sending word to the neighbours.

The **Irish harp**, though it remained the symbolic instrument of Ireland and appeared on coins and official seals, was by the 1820s in steep and seemingly terminal decline as a living tradition. The great **Belfast Harp Festival of 1792** had been organized precisely because its promoters — among them the United Irishman Edward Bunting — recognized that the tradition was dying. Bunting transcribed the music of the aged harpers who attended, some of them blind, most of them the last of their line. By the 1820s, the wire-strung harp played with long fingernails in the old aristocratic style was essentially extinct as a performance tradition. The harp's decline was bound up with the destruction of the Gaelic aristocratic order that had patronized harpers for centuries. In a game set in Kilteevan, the harp would be a memory and a symbol rather than a living presence — something an elderly character might speak of with reverence, recalling a harper heard in youth.

The **wooden flute** and the **tin whistle** were also present in the musical landscape, though the flute's great era in Irish traditional music would come somewhat later in the nineteenth century, as cheap wooden concert flutes became widely available. The tin whistle, simple and affordable, was ubiquitous as a learning instrument and a casual one. The **bodhran**, the goatskin frame drum now iconic in Irish music, presents a historical puzzle. There is genuine scholarly debate about whether the bodhran was commonly used as a musical instrument this early, or whether its widespread adoption in sessions came later. It may have existed in some form as a rhythmic accompaniment at wren-day processions and other folk events, but it would be anachronistic to place it prominently in every musical gathering of the 1820s.

### Song, Story, and the Voice Alone

**Sean-nos singing** — literally "old style" — was the vocal heart of Irish musical tradition. This was unaccompanied solo singing in the Irish language, highly ornamented with melismatic turns and grace notes, deeply emotional in delivery, and governed by aesthetic conventions quite unlike those of European art music. A sean-nos singer did not project outward to a concert hall; they sang inward, drawing listeners into an intimate space. The ornaments were not decorative flourishes but structural elements, and each singer was expected to bring their own variations to well-known songs. In a Connacht parish in the 1820s, where Irish was still the primary language of daily life for most of the population, sean-nos singing would have been a regular feature of evening gatherings, wakes, and festivals. The songs themselves carried layers of meaning — love songs, laments, political allegory, religious devotion — and a knowledgeable audience could read these layers with sophistication.

The **seanchai** (storyteller) was a revered figure in rural Irish society, a keeper of oral history, mythology, genealogy, and local lore. The seanchai's art was performance: voice modulation, timing, gesture, audience interaction. A skilled storyteller could hold a roomful of people spellbound for hours, moving between tales of the Fianna, local ghost stories, accounts of historical events, and the intricate genealogies that told people who they were and where they came from. In a society where literacy in English was limited and literacy in Irish almost nonexistent (Irish being primarily an oral language at this date), the seanchai served as a living library. Storytelling evenings by the fire were the **primary winter entertainment** in rural Ireland. When the agricultural work slowed, when darkness came early and the cold kept people indoors, families and neighbours gathered around the hearth, and the night belonged to stories. This was not mere diversion — it was the mechanism by which culture, identity, and communal memory were transmitted across generations.

The tradition of the **travelling musician** deserves particular mention. Itinerant players — pipers, fiddlers, sometimes harpers in earlier decades — moved between houses and parishes, trading music for food, lodging, and whatever small payment might be offered. They carried tunes and news from one district to another, serving as a living network of cultural exchange. A travelling musician arriving in Kilteevan would bring new tunes from Galway or Sligo, stories from the road, and a welcome break from routine.

### Gatherings, Dancing, and Social Life

**Set dancing** was the dominant social dance form, derived from **French quadrilles** that had filtered down through the Anglo-Irish gentry and been enthusiastically adopted and adapted by the rural population. Sets were danced by four couples in a square formation, but the movements were performed to Irish music — reels, jigs, polkas, hornpipes — giving them a character entirely distinct from their Continental origins. Each locality developed its own variations, so that the sets danced in Kilteevan would have differed in their figures and timing from those danced even a few parishes away.

**Crossroads dances** were the great social institution of summer evenings. When the weather was fine and the evenings long, young people gathered at a convenient crossroads — often one with a flat, hard-packed surface suitable for dancing — and danced to the music of a local fiddler or piper. These were the primary venue for courtship and socializing among young people, and they carried an energy and freedom that the Catholic clergy frequently viewed with suspicion. The crossroads dance was outdoor, informal, unsupervised by elders in any strict sense, and it brought young men and women together in physical proximity. For the game's purposes, crossroads dances represent a crucial social mechanic — a place where relationships form, rivalries play out, and the community's youth culture is visible.

**Pattern days** — from "patron," referring to a parish's patron saint — were festivals that combined religious devotion with exuberant secular entertainment. A pattern day typically involved a pilgrimage to a holy well or other sacred site, rounds of prayer, and then an extended period of music, dancing, drinking, athletic contests, and frequently fighting. Faction fights between rival groups — whether based on family, townland, or parish allegiance — were a notorious feature of pattern days, and the authorities and clergy alike spent much of the early nineteenth century trying to suppress them. A pattern day in Kilteevan would have been the social and entertainment highlight of the calendar, a day when the entire parish came together and normal rules were loosened.

### Sport, Games, and Blood

**Hurling** was the ancient Gaelic sport, played between teams representing townlands or parishes, fast and rough, using a wooden stick (the camán or hurley) and a leather ball (the sliotar). Matches in the 1820s were loosely organized affairs with large numbers of players on each side and few standardized rules — the formal codification of hurling under the GAA would not come until 1884. A hurling match between Kilteevan and a neighbouring parish would have been a major event, wrapped up in local pride and fierce rivalry.

**Blood sports** were common and not yet subject to legal prohibition. **Cockfighting** was widespread, with birds bred and trained for the purpose and significant sums wagered on the outcome. **Bull-baiting** and **dog fighting** also occurred, particularly at fairs and pattern days. These activities were part of the normal fabric of rural entertainment, viewed without the moral disapproval that later generations would attach to them.

**Card games** were a staple of indoor entertainment, especially **"Twenty-Five"** (also called Spoil Five), a trick-taking game with deep roots in Ireland that remained the dominant card game in rural areas well into the twentieth century. Gambling was endemic — on cards, on sport, on cockfights, on anything that offered a contest and an outcome. Fairs were particular hotspots for gambling, with card sharps, thimbleriggers, and other operators working the crowds.

### Death and Celebration: Music at Wakes

The **Irish wake** was one of the most distinctive cultural institutions, and music played a central role in it. **Keening** — **caoineadh** in Irish — was the women's lament for the dead, a formalized expression of grief performed by female relatives and sometimes by professional keening women (mna caointe). Keening was part wail, part song, part poetry, an improvised oral performance that praised the dead, expressed the community's loss, and channelled grief into a structured form. The Catholic clergy spent decades trying to suppress keening, viewing it as a pagan survival, but in the 1820s it remained a living practice.

Yet the wake was far more than mourning. After the keening and prayers, the wake often became a lively social gathering with music, dancing, storytelling, games (some of them bawdy), abundant food and drink (especially whiskey and tobacco), and an atmosphere that outsiders frequently found shockingly festive. This was not disrespect for the dead but a deeply held cultural understanding that death was to be met with community, vitality, and an affirmation of life's continuity.

### Game Design Implications

Music and entertainment offer rich possibilities for Parish as an interactive fiction experience. **Music events** should function as dynamic social gatherings that the player can attend, participate in, or overhear — a crossroads dance on a summer evening, a storytelling session by the fire on a winter night, a piper arriving at the local public house. These events serve as natural hubs for NPC interaction, information exchange, and relationship development.

**NPC musicians and storytellers** — a parish fiddler, a visiting piper, a resident seanchai — should be distinct character types with their own social roles and schedules. The travelling musician mechanic offers a natural way to introduce news, new characters, and plot developments from outside the parish. A seanchai NPC could serve as a vehicle for delivering lore, backstory, and cultural context to the player in a way that feels organic to the setting.

A **seasonal entertainment calendar** should govern what activities are available: crossroads dances in summer, storytelling evenings in winter, pattern days on fixed saint's days, hurling matches on Sundays and holidays, wakes whenever death occurs. This calendar reinforces the game's time system and gives the player reasons to engage with the passage of seasons.

**Wake mechanics** deserve particular attention, as they combine mourning, socializing, music, and community bonding into a single multi-phase event. A wake could involve keening (emotional, solemn), followed by storytelling and music (lively, communal), with opportunities for the player to interact with NPCs in both registers. The wake is one of the few social institutions that brings the entire community together regardless of faction or status, making it a uniquely rich space for narrative and relationship development.

Finally, **blood sports and gambling** provide morally textured content — activities that were normal and accepted in the 1820s but uncomfortable by modern standards. The game can present these without anachronistic judgment while allowing the player to form their own responses, adding authenticity and moral complexity to the world of Kilteevan.
