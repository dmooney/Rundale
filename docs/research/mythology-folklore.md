# Mythology & Folklore in 1820s Ireland

## The Living Fairy Faith

To understand rural Ireland in the 1820s, one must grasp a central fact: the **fairy faith** was not a quaint superstition clinging to life at the margins of belief. It was a living, breathing cosmology that shaped how ordinary people moved through the landscape, planted their crops, raised their children, and buried their dead. The fairies of Irish tradition bore no resemblance to the tiny, winged creatures that Victorian England would later popularize. The Irish **sídhe** (pronounced roughly "shee") [1] — the people of the mounds — were powerful, capricious, and deeply dangerous. They were understood to be a race apart, neither angelic nor demonic but something older and stranger, inhabiting a parallel world that pressed close against the mortal one. A farmer in Kilteevan, County Roscommon, would have spoken of the **Good People** or the **Gentry** with genuine respect and genuine fear, using these euphemisms precisely because naming them directly was considered reckless.

## The Tuatha Dé Danann and the Otherworld

The mythological foundations of the fairy faith ran deep. In the old stories, still told around hearth fires in the 1820s, the **Tuatha Dé Danann** — the People of the Goddess Danu — were a semi-divine race who once ruled Ireland. Masters of magic, art, and craft, they were defeated by the mortal Milesians and retreated not into exile but into the land itself, taking up residence inside the hollow hills and ancient mounds that dotted the countryside [2]. Over centuries, these mythic beings became the fairies of folk belief, diminished in stature in some tellings but never in power. The world they inhabited was the **Otherworld** — not heaven, not hell, not purgatory, but a parallel realm of strange beauty and terrible danger. It was a place where time moved differently, where a night of dancing might cost a mortal seven years, where the food was enchanted and the music unearthly. The Otherworld was not distant. It pressed close at certain places — through **fairy forts**, beneath lakes, within mists that gathered in low hollows — and at certain times, the boundary between worlds grew thin enough to cross.

## Fairy Forts and the Sacred Landscape

Scattered across the Roscommon landscape were the physical anchors of this belief: the **raths** or **ring forts**, ancient circular earthworks mostly dating to the Early Medieval period (c. 500–900 CE), with some older examples from the Late Iron Age [3]. By the 1820s, their original purpose as farmsteads and defensive enclosures had been entirely forgotten. To the people who lived beside them, they were **fairy forts** — the dwelling places and gathering grounds of the sídhe. The prohibition against disturbing them was absolute and deeply felt. No farmer, however desperate for land, would plow through a rath. No landlord's agent, however powerful, could easily compel tenants to level one. Trees growing on fairy forts — especially the **fairy thorn** (hawthorn) — were inviolable. Stories circulated constantly of those who had defied these taboos: the man who cut a fairy thorn and whose cattle sickened, the family who built over a fairy path and suffered mysterious illnesses, the landlord who ordered a rath levelled and died within the year. These stories were not told as ancient legends but as recent, local events, confirmed by neighbours who knew the people involved.

## Changelings, Banshees, and the Púca

Among the most feared manifestations of fairy power was the **changeling** [4]. It was widely believed that the sídhe coveted human children — particularly healthy, beautiful infants — and would steal them away to the Otherworld, leaving in their place a **fairy substitute**: a sickly, wizened creature that wailed constantly and failed to thrive. The changeling belief provided a framework for understanding sudden childhood illness, developmental disability, and the terrible infant mortality that haunted pre-Famine Ireland. Certain tests were believed to reveal a changeling — placing it on a shovel over the fire, dosing it with foxglove tea, or leaving it briefly on a fairy fort [5] — and these rituals, heartbreaking in hindsight, were carried out with desperate sincerity by parents who believed their real child might yet be recovered.

The **banshee** — from **bean sídhe**, the fairy woman [6] — occupied a different role in the supernatural landscape. She was not malevolent but mournful, a spectral woman whose keening wail foretold the death of a member of certain old Irish families. To hear the banshee was to know that death was coming, and while terrifying, her cry carried a strange dignity — it meant your family was old enough and significant enough to have a banshee attached to it. In Roscommon, particular families claimed their own banshee, and accounts of her cry preceding a death were treated as straightforward fact, not legend.

The **púca** (sometimes anglicized as pooka) was altogether more unpredictable — a **shapeshifting trickster spirit** who might appear as a black horse, a goat, a hare, or a dark, rough-featured man [7]. The púca haunted lonely roads and wild places, and an encounter with it might be merely unsettling or genuinely dangerous. It could lead travellers astray, spoil crops left unharvested after Samhain (which belonged to the púca by right) [8], or offer terrifying midnight rides to those unlucky enough to encounter it in horse form. Yet the púca was not purely malicious — in some traditions it could be helpful, even prophetic, and a wise person knew to leave it a share of the harvest rather than risk its displeasure.

## Fairy Doctors and Pisheogs

Not everyone was helpless before the fairy world. **Fairy doctors** — sometimes called **fairy men** or **fairy women** — were individuals believed to possess special knowledge of fairy ways, often acquired through their own abduction to the Otherworld or through a gift passed down within certain families [9]. They served as healers and intermediaries, diagnosing fairy-caused illnesses (a cow that stopped giving milk, a child who wasted away, a man struck with sudden paralysis) and prescribing cures that combined herbal medicine with ritual actions. A fairy doctor might instruct a client to leave offerings at a fairy fort, to turn their coat inside out when passing a dangerous place, or to use specific herbs gathered at specific times. Their authority was considerable and their role distinct from both the Catholic priest and the ordinary herbalist, though it overlapped with both.

Closely related was the world of **pisheogs** — a broad term encompassing folk magic, curses, and protective charms. Pisheogs could be benign (a charm to protect butter-making, a prayer spoken over a sick animal) or malicious (a curse laid on a neighbour's land, an enchantment to steal another household's luck). The fear of pisheogs was pervasive, and accusations of practising malicious folk magic could destroy social relationships. Protective measures were woven into daily life: iron hung over doorways to ward off fairies, rowan crosses placed in byres, salt scattered on thresholds, and specific prayers recited when undertaking any venture that might attract supernatural attention.

## Liminal Spaces and Thin Times

The fairy faith was fundamentally a geography of power. Certain places were understood as **liminal** — thresholds between the mortal world and the Otherworld. **Crossroads**, where paths intersected, were places where worlds met and where supernatural encounters were most likely. **Bogs** were uncanny, dangerous, otherworldly spaces where the normal rules of the landscape broke down, where lights flickered without source, and where the unwary could be swallowed whole. **Holy wells**, many of them pre-Christian sacred sites later absorbed into Catholic practice, were places of healing and petition, where pilgrims left offerings of rags, pins, and coins, and where the water was believed to cure specific ailments. Even time had its thin places: **dawn and dusk** — the in-between hours — were moments when the boundary weakened and encounters with the sídhe were most likely.

## The Four Festivals

The liturgical year of fairy belief was marked by four great seasonal festivals, each ancient, each carrying layers of meaning that stretched back far beyond Christianity. **Samhain** (roughly 1 November) [10] was the most potent — the night when the boundary between worlds dissolved entirely, when the dead walked, when the sídhe rode out in force, and when divination was most powerful. **Imbolc** (1 February) honoured **Brigid**, a figure who straddled the line between pagan goddess and Christian saint [11], and marked the first stirrings of spring. **Bealtaine** (1 May) was a festival of fire and fertility, when cattle were driven between bonfires for purification before being sent to summer pasture [12], and when the fairy world was especially active and dangerous. **Lughnasa** (1 August) celebrated the harvest and honoured Lugh, the master of all skills [13], with gatherings, games, and the first fruits of the season. Each festival carried specific rituals, prohibitions, and dangers, and each was observed in the 1820s with a blend of Christian and pre-Christian practice that most people saw no contradiction in.

## Faith and Fairy: A Comfortable Coexistence

This brings us to a crucial point: the relationship between **Catholic faith and fairy belief** in 1820s Ireland was not one of opposition but of **comfortable coexistence**. The institutional Church might disapprove, and individual priests might rail against fairy superstition from the altar, but for the vast majority of ordinary people, the two belief systems occupied different territories and answered different questions. God and the saints governed the soul, morality, and the afterlife. The fairies governed the land, luck, health, and the uncanny. A woman might attend Mass devoutly on Sunday and leave an offering at a fairy fort on Monday without any sense of contradiction. Holy water was as effective against fairy mischief as against the devil, and certain saints — Brigid foremost among them — had roots so deep in pre-Christian tradition that they served as bridges between the two worlds of belief.

The keeper and transmitter of these traditions was the **seanchaí** (pronounced "shan-a-key") [14] — the storyteller, a figure of enormous cultural importance in a society where literacy was limited and oral tradition was the primary vehicle of knowledge, history, and identity. The seanchaí did not merely entertain, though entertainment was part of their art. They preserved genealogies, local history, legal precedent, and the vast body of mythological and folkloric knowledge that gave the community its sense of place and meaning. In the 1820s, the seanchaí tradition was still vigorous, though the pressures of anglicization, the decline of the Irish language, and the coming catastrophe of the Famine would soon deal it devastating blows.

## Game Design Implications

The mythology and folklore of 1820s Ireland offer extraordinarily rich material for Parish. A **mythology layer** could operate beneath the surface of the game's realism, never confirming or denying the supernatural but allowing players to encounter the world as its inhabitants experienced it — ambiguous, charged with meaning, shaped by belief. **Fairy fort locations** on the parish map should function as significant landmarks with associated stories, prohibitions, and NPC attitudes; a player who disturbs a rath should face social consequences from horrified neighbours, and strange events might follow that admit of both natural and supernatural explanation.

**Seasonal events** tied to the four festivals offer natural narrative beats: Samhain gatherings with divination games and ghost stories, Bealtaine bonfires with their rituals and revelry, Lughnasa fairs with competitions and courtship. Each season should subtly shift NPC behaviour, dialogue, and the atmospheric tone of the game world. NPCs should hold and express **beliefs and superstitions** that vary by character — a devout older woman who leaves milk out for the fairies, a young man educated at a hedge school who is sceptical but still won't cut a fairy thorn, a fairy doctor whom people visit quietly and never discuss openly, a priest who campaigns against superstition but privately admits he has heard the banshee.

**Liminal spaces** — crossroads, bogs, holy wells, the edges of fairy forts at dusk — should carry distinct atmospheric weight in descriptions, with language that becomes more uncertain, more charged, more suggestive at these thresholds. The **seanchaí** is a natural NPC archetype: a source of lore, local history, and narrative depth, whose storytelling sessions could deliver mythology to the player in a way that feels organic rather than expository. The changeling belief, the banshee, the púca, and the world of pisheogs all offer hooks for quests, moral dilemmas, and encounters that test the player's relationship with the community and its deepest convictions. The key design principle should be **ambiguity**: the game world should feel as it felt to its real inhabitants, where the rational and the uncanny were not separate categories but woven together in the fabric of everyday life.

## Fact-Check Notes

[1] **Verified.** The pronunciation of *sídhe* as roughly "shee" is correct. The word *sídhe* (modern Irish *sí*) means "fairy mound" and by extension the fairy folk themselves. Pronounced /ʃiː/ in IPA. The term *aos sí* ("people of the mounds") is the more technically correct name for the fairy folk, while *sídhe* properly refers to the mounds. — Wikipedia, "Aos Si"; The Irish Pagan School, "What is a Sidhe?"

[2] **Verified.** The Tuatha De Danann were defeated by the Milesians at the Battle of Tailteann and retreated into the sídhe mounds. According to the medieval *Lebor Gabala Erenn*, Amergin divided Ireland: the portion above ground to the Milesians, the portion underground to the Tuatha De Danann. They were then led underground by Manannan mac Lir. The description of them as "People of the Goddess Danu" and "semi-divine" is consistent with scholarly sources. — Wikipedia, "Tuatha De Danann"; Mythopedia.

[3] **Corrected.** The original text described ring forts as "dating back thousands of years to the Iron Age and earlier." This is misleading. Radiocarbon dating shows that over 50% of excavated ring forts date from 540–884 CE (Early Medieval period), and two-thirds date from 600–900 CE. While some sites have older origins and a minority may date to the Late Iron Age (c. 100–500 CE), characterizing them broadly as Iron Age monuments is inaccurate. The text has been corrected to reflect the Early Medieval dating with some Iron Age examples. — Wikipedia, "Ringfort"; Monumental Ireland.

[4] **Verified.** The changeling belief -- that fairies would steal healthy human children and leave a sickly substitute -- is extensively documented in Irish folklore. The belief provided a framework for understanding childhood illness and developmental disability. — Wikipedia, "Changeling"; Skeptical Inquirer, "The Enduring Legend of the Changeling."

[5] **Verified.** The tests described -- placing the suspected changeling on a hot shovel over a fire, dosing with foxglove, and exposure at fairy forts -- are all documented in folklore sources. The shovel-and-fire test is one of the most widely attested. Foxglove (digitalis) baths are documented particularly in Donegal cases from the 1870s–1890s. These rituals sometimes resulted in injury or death, as in the Bridget Cleary case (1895). — Wikipedia, "Changeling"; Carole Silver's research on changeling cases.

[6] **Verified.** *Bean sídhe* (modern *bean sí*) translates as "woman of the fairy mound." The banshee is documented as a death omen associated with certain old Irish families. Accounts reach back to at least 1380 (*Cathreim Thoirdhealbhaigh*). Traditionally associated with families of "pure Milesian stock" including the O'Neills, O'Briens, O'Connors, O'Gradys, and Kavanaghs, though intermarriage extended the list. — Wikipedia, "Banshee"; Britannica, "Banshee."

[7] **Verified.** The puca is documented as a shapeshifting creature most commonly appearing as a black horse, goat, dog, or hare, and sometimes as a dark-featured human. Its fur is described as almost always dark. The characterization as a trickster spirit is consistent with sources. Regional variations include a goblin form in County Down, a bogeyman in County Laois, and notably a black goat in Roscommon. — Wikipedia, "Puca"; various Irish folklore sources.

[8] **Verified.** The association between the puca and unharvested crops after Samhain is well attested. Any crops remaining in the fields after 1 November were considered "the Pooka's share" and inedible. This taboo is widely documented across Irish folklore sources. — Wikipedia, "Puca"; Irish Central, "The Irish legend of the Pooka."

[9] **Verified.** Fairy doctors are documented as intermediaries between the human and fairy worlds, gaining their knowledge through illness/"abduction" experiences, family inheritance, or fairy encounters. They diagnosed fairy-caused ailments and prescribed cures combining herbal medicine with ritual actions. Their role was distinct from but overlapping with priests and herbalists. Notable examples include Biddy Early (19th century, County Clare). — Lady Wilde, *Ancient Legends of Ireland*; various folklore sources.

[10] **Verified.** Samhain (1 November, celebrations beginning evening of 31 October) is confirmed as one of four Gaelic seasonal festivals. It marked the end of the harvest season and beginning of the "darker half" of the year. It was considered the time when the boundary between the living and the dead was most permeable. — Wikipedia, "Samhain"; Nine Arrow, "Exploring the Ancient Irish Festivals."

[11] **Verified.** Imbolc (1 February) is confirmed as honouring Brigid. The goddess Brigid of the Tuatha De Danann (associated with poetry, healing, and smithcraft) and Saint Brigid of Kildare (5th/6th century) have been extensively intertwined, with scholars noting the saint likely absorbed attributes of the goddess. The festival is historically linked to the beginning of the lambing season. — Wikipedia, "Imbolc"; Wikipedia, "Brigid."

[12] **Verified.** Bealtaine (1 May) is confirmed as a fire festival marking the beginning of summer. The practice of driving cattle between two bonfires for purification before sending them to summer pastures is well attested, documented by 17th-century historian Geoffrey Keating and observed as late as the 19th century. The Irish expression *idir dha thine Bhealtaine* ("between the two fires of Bealtaine") derives from this practice. — Wikipedia, "Beltane"; RTE Brainstorm.

[13] **Verified.** Lughnasa (1 August) is confirmed as a harvest festival honouring Lugh. Lugh's epithet *Samildanach* ("equally skilled in many arts") confirms the "master of all skills" description. The festival was instituted by Lugh in memory of his foster mother Tailtiu and featured athletic contests (Tailteann Games), feasting, matchmaking, and trading. — Wikipedia, "Lughnasadh"; Mythopedia, "Lugh."

[14] **Verified.** The pronunciation "shan-a-key" for *seanchai* is confirmed (IPA: /ˈʃan̪ˠəxiː/). The seanchai served as storyteller, historian, and custodian of oral tradition including genealogies, local history, legal precedent, and mythological knowledge. The role is understood as an informal successor to the bardic tradition of pre-Christian Ireland. — Wikipedia, "Seanchai"; Irish Central, "Seanchai: The storytelling keepers of Ireland's heritage."
