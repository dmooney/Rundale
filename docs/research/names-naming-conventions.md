Names in 1820s Ireland were not simple labels. They were compressed genealogies, tribal affiliations, geographic markers, and social signals all at once. Understanding how people were named, how they named their children, and how those names were mangled, translated, and fought over by competing cultures is essential to building a world that feels inhabited by real people rather than interchangeable peasant sprites.

**The Gaelic surname system** was built on descent. The two great prefixes — **Mac** (meaning "son of") and **Ó** (meaning "grandson of" or, more broadly, "descendant of") [1] — encoded a person's lineage directly into their name. Mac Diarmada meant "son of Diarmuid"; Ó Briain meant "descendant of Brian." These were not arbitrary family names in the modern sense but living references to a specific ancestor, usually a chieftain or notable figure from whom the family claimed descent. The Mac prefix pointed to a closer, more recent connection — the son or immediate line of a named father — while the Ó prefix indicated a deeper, more ancient lineage, typically tracing back to a grandfather or earlier progenitor who had established the family's identity. Over centuries, these patronymics fossilised into hereditary surnames, but in the 1820s, particularly in Irish-speaking communities, the living awareness of what these prefixes meant had not entirely faded. A man named Ó Murchú knew, or could be told by any seanchaí worth his salt, that the name traced back to a sea-warrior ancestor, *Murchadh*, "sea-battler" [2].

**English authorities anglicized Irish names** through a process that was part administrative convenience, part cultural erasure, and part sheer phonetic incomprehension. English clerks, soldiers, landlords, and later census-takers heard Irish names spoken aloud and wrote down whatever approximation their English-trained ears could manage. **Ó Briain** became **O'Brien**. **Ó Murchú** became **Murphy** — the Ó dropped entirely, the Irish sounds flattened into something an English tongue could manage. **Mac Diarmada** became **McDermott**. **Ó Ceallaigh** became **Kelly**, shedding its prefix along the way, as many names did — the "O" and "Mac" were frequently dropped during the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries, sometimes under legal pressure from the Statutes of Kilkenny [3] and later Penal-era conventions that penalised Gaelic customs, sometimes simply through the gravitational pull of anglicisation. By the 1820s, you could find families who still used the prefix and families who had abandoned it, sometimes within the same parish. A man might sign a lease as "Patrick Kelly" and be known among his neighbours as "Pádraig Ó Ceallaigh," and neither he nor anyone around him would find this contradictory. The two names coexisted, one for the English-speaking world of law and commerce, the other for the Irish-speaking world of home and community.

**Patronymic chains** were the older and more precise system of identification, and they survived robustly in Irish-speaking areas well into the nineteenth century. Rather than relying solely on a fixed surname, a person was identified by their father and often their grandfather, creating a chain that located them precisely within the web of local kinship. A man might be known as **Seán Mór mac Pádraig**, "Big Seán son of Patrick," or more elaborately as **Seán mac Pádraig mac Thomáis**, specifying three generations. Women were identified similarly, using **ní** (daughter of, from *iníon*) or **bean** (wife of) [4]: **Máire ní Bheirn**, "Mary daughter of Beirne," or **Máire bean Uí Cheallaigh**, "Mary wife of Kelly." These chains were not bureaucratic — no clerk recorded them in a ledger — but they were socially essential. In a parish where a quarter of the men might be named Seán or Pádraig, and where three or four families might share the surname Kelly, the patronymic chain was how you knew exactly which Seán Kelly was being discussed. The system was oral, carried in the community's collective memory, and it served as a kind of living census far more granular than anything the state would attempt until the Ordnance Survey.

**The common surnames of Connacht and Roscommon** reflect the region's specific history of settlement, chieftainship, and tribal territory. **Kelly** (Ó Ceallaigh) was then and remains among the most common surnames in Ireland [5], and it was densely concentrated in Connacht. **Beirne** (Ó Beirn), an old Roscommon family, was firmly rooted in the area around Kilteevan and Strokestown. **Murray** (Ó Muireadhaigh), **Flanagan** (Ó Flannagáin), **McDermott** (Mac Diarmada), and **Hanley** (Ó hÁinle) were all prominent in the county. **McDermott** carried particular weight: the Mac Diarmada had been the ruling family of Moylurg [6], a Gaelic lordship centred on the Boyle area of north Roscommon, and the name still carried echoes of that old authority even after centuries of dispossession. **Brennan** (Ó Braonáin), **Quinn** (Ó Cuinn), **Regan** (Ó Riagáin), and **Connolly** (Ó Conghaile) were also well represented. The density of shared surnames in any given townland was remarkable — a single cluster of houses might contain three unrelated Kelly families and two Beirne households, all of whom needed to be distinguished from one another by every means available.

**First names** in the 1820s existed in a state of bilingual doubling that mirrored the surname situation. The Irish forms — **Seán**, **Pádraig**, **Micheál**, **Tomás**, **Diarmuid** for men; **Máire**, **Bríd** (Brigid), **Síle**, **Caitríona**, **Nóra**, **Eibhlín** for women — were the names used at home, in Irish conversation, and in the daily life of the community. The anglicised equivalents — **John**, **Patrick**, **Michael**, **Thomas**, **Jeremiah** (a common anglicisation of Diarmuid); **Mary**, **Bridget**, **Sheila**, **Catherine**, **Nora**, **Eileen** — were the forms used in church registers, legal documents, and interactions with English-speaking authority. The correspondence between Irish and English forms was sometimes straightforward (Seán/John, Máire/Mary) and sometimes wildly approximate: **Diarmuid** became **Jeremiah** or even **Darby** [7] through phonetic drift rather than translation, and **Gobnait**, an Irish women's name honouring a sixth-century saint [8], was often rendered as **Deborah** or **Abigail** — names with no etymological connection whatsoever, chosen simply because English clerks needed to write something recognisable.

**Naming children** followed deeply conservative patterns rooted in family obligation and religious devotion. The most widespread convention was **grandparent naming** [9]: the first son was named after the father's father, the second son after the mother's father, the first daughter after the mother's mother, the second daughter after the father's mother. Subsequent children might be named after uncles, aunts, or the patron saint of the parish. **Saint's names** dominated — Patrick, Brigid, Michael, Mary — reinforcing the Catholic identity of the community. The result was an extraordinary concentration of identical names. A parish like Kilteevan might contain dozens of men named Seán or Pádraig and dozens of women named Máire or Bríd, all distinguished only by surname, patronymic, or byname. When infant mortality claimed a child, the name was frequently reused for the next child of the same sex, meaning a single family might have two or three successive children baptised with the same name, only one of whom survived. Parish registers from the period are full of these ghostly repetitions.

**Nicknames and bynames** were the community's practical solution to the confusion generated by this naming conservatism. Physical descriptors were the most common: **Rua** (red-haired), **Mór** (big, or senior), **Óg** (young, or junior), **Bán** (fair/white-haired), **Dubh** (dark), **Beag** (small), **Caoch** (blind or squinting). Occupational identifiers served the same purpose: Seán an Gabha (Seán the smith), Pádraig an tSiúinéara (Patrick the carpenter). **Locational bynames** tied a person to their townland or a specific landscape feature: Tomás an Chnoic (Thomas of the hill), Máire na hAbhann (Mary of the river). These bynames were essential social technology — without them, daily life in a community dense with shared names would have been unnavigable. They were used constantly in speech, understood by everyone locally, and almost never appeared in any written record, which means they survive only in oral tradition and occasional anecdote.

**Townland names** are among the richest veins of the Irish language still visible on the modern landscape, and in the 1820s they were even more transparently meaningful. Nearly every townland name in Roscommon is an anglicised rendering of an Irish original that described the land, commemorated a saint, or recorded a historical event. **Kilteevan** (*Cill Taobháin*) means "Teevan's Church" [10] — *cill* being one of the most common elements in Irish place names, marking an early Christian church site. **Strokestown** is an English coinage, but the surrounding townlands carry names like **Cloonfad** (*Cluain Fada*, "long meadow") [11], **Lissonuffy** (*Lios Ó nDubhthaigh*, "the fort of the O'Duffys") [12], and **Tulsk** (*Tulach*, "hillock") [13]. Common elements recurring across the county include **baile** (town, homestead), **ráth** (ringfort), **lios** (fort enclosure), **dún** (stronghold), **druim** (ridge), **carraig** (rock), and **achadh** (field). These names were not historical curiosities — they were the living, working vocabulary of the landscape, spoken daily by people who still understood their meaning.

**The anglicisation of place names** reached its most systematic and consequential phase just after the period Parish is set in, with the **Ordnance Survey of Ireland** launched in 1824 [14]. Brian Friel's play *Translations*, set in a Donegal hedge school in 1833 [15], dramatises this process with painful clarity: English sappers and engineers arrive in an Irish-speaking community to map the landscape, and in doing so they translate, transliterate, or simply replace Irish place names with English equivalents. What Friel captured — and what was historically real — was that this was not a neutral act of cartography but a profound act of cultural appropriation. To rename a place was to sever it from the web of meaning, memory, and story that its Irish name carried. The Ordnance Survey officers, particularly the brilliant Irish scholar **John O'Donovan** [16], made extraordinary efforts to record the original Irish forms, but the end product was a set of official anglicised names that became the only versions recognised by law, administration, and eventually common usage. In the 1820s, this process was just beginning, and the Irish-speaking population still used Irish names as a matter of course. The English forms existed — landlords' maps and estate records used them — but they had not yet acquired the crushing finality that the Ordnance Survey would impose.

**Women's names at marriage** followed a pattern shaped by the collision of Irish and English custom. In the older Irish tradition, a woman did not automatically take her husband's surname. She might be known as **Máire ní Fhlannagáin** (Mary daughter of Flanagan) throughout her life, or identified as **bean Uí Bheirn** (wife of Beirne) when the context demanded it, but her identity was not subsumed into her husband's name in the way English convention required. The English practice of a woman fully adopting her husband's surname — becoming "Mrs. Kelly" rather than remaining "Mary Flanagan" — was penetrating Irish life through the legal system, church records, and the general pressure of anglicisation, but in daily Irish-language usage, women were still commonly identified by their birth families. This created yet another layer of dual naming: a woman might be "Mrs. Patrick Murray" in the parish register and "Bríd ní Cheallaigh" on the lips of her neighbours.

## Game Design Implications

This research has direct consequences for how Parish names and addresses its NPCs. Every character needs at minimum two forms of their name — an Irish form and an anglicised form — and the choice of which form appears in dialogue should depend on context: who is speaking, what language they are using, and what social register the conversation occupies. Characters should also carry **patronymic identifiers** and **bynames** that the community uses to distinguish the inevitable clusters of people sharing the same given name and surname. When a player enters Kilteevan and meets three men named Seán Kelly, the game should make clear — through dialogue, NPC introductions, and ambient speech — that one is Seán Rua (red-haired Seán), one is Seán Mór mac Pádraig (Big Seán son of Patrick), and one is Seán an Mhuilinn (Seán of the mill). These distinguishing markers are not flavour text; they are the actual social technology by which an 1820s Irish community functioned.

Location names should similarly exist in dual form, with Irish-speaking NPCs naturally using the Irish name and English-speaking or formal contexts using the anglicised version. The NPC dialogue system should reflect the naming conventions around women — a female NPC might be introduced differently depending on whether the speaker is using Irish or English convention, and whether they identify her through her birth family or her husband's name.

For the player's experience of addressing NPCs, the game should allow both forms and be forgiving about which one the player uses, while having NPCs occasionally correct or contextualise the choice — a small but atmospheric way to make the bilingual world feel alive. The sheer density of shared names, managed through bynames and patronymics, is itself a storytelling opportunity: it forces the player to learn the community the way the community knows itself, not through surnames on a list but through the web of relationships, physical traits, occupations, and places that make each person distinct.

## Fact-Check Notes

[1] **Verified.** "Mac" literally means "son of" and was used to indicate patrilineal descent. "Ó" (older form "Ua") means "grandson" or more broadly "descendant of." Both eventually became fixed hereditary surnames. — Britannica, "Mac (surname prefix)."

[2] **Verified.** Murchadh is composed of "muir" (sea) and "cath" (battle), making it "sea-battler" or "sea-warrior." The anglicised form Murphy derives from both Ó Murchadha and Mac Murchadha. — Wikipedia, "Murchadh."

[3] **Verified.** The 1366 Statutes of Kilkenny explicitly prohibited the use of Irish names and naming conventions, requiring English families to abandon traditional Irish naming practices among many other Gaelic customs. — Britannica, "Statute of Kilkenny."

[4] **Verified.** "Ní" is a contraction of "Iníon Uí" meaning "descendant's daughter," while "Bean Uí" means "descendant's wife." These prefixes encode both gender and marital status in the Irish surname system. — Wikipedia, "Irish name."

[5] **Corrected.** Murphy is currently the most common surname in Ireland, with Kelly as the second most common. The original text stated Kelly was the most common. When all spelling variations are combined, Kelly remains among the top two. — Ireland Before You Die, "Top 100 Irish Surnames."

[6] **Verified.** The Mac Diarmada ruled Moylurg from the 10th to 16th centuries. Their chief fortress was McDermott's Castle on an island in Lough Key near Boyle. — Wikipedia, "Mac Diarmada."

[7] **Verified.** Diarmaid has historically been anglicised as Jeremiah (or Jeremy), though the names are etymologically unrelated. Darby is also a documented anglicised form, along with Dermot and Dermod. — Library Ireland, "DIARMAID (Irish Names and Surnames)."

[8] **Verified.** Gobnait flourished in the 5th or 6th century. Her name was anglicised as both Abigail and Deborah — Abigail matching the Irish meaning ("brings joy") and Deborah referencing her association with beekeeping. She is the patron saint of bees and beekeepers. — Wikipedia, "Gobnait."

[9] **Verified.** The traditional Irish naming pattern was: first son after the paternal grandfather, second son after the maternal grandfather, third son after the father. For daughters: first daughter after the maternal grandmother, second after the paternal grandmother, third after the mother. These conventions were widely used until the late 19th century. — FamilySearch, "Ireland Naming Customs."

[10] **Corrected.** The Irish name for Kilteevan is *Cill Taobháin* (not *Cill Tíobáin*), meaning "Teevan's Church." The spelling and saint attribution have been corrected to match the form confirmed in the Irish Language research document. — Wikipedia, "Kilteevan"; townlands.ie.

[11] **Verified.** Cloonfad derives from Irish "Cluain Fada," with "cluain" meaning meadow or pasture and "fada" meaning long. The village is in County Roscommon. — Wikipedia, "Cloonfad."

[12] **Corrected.** The Irish name is *Lios Ó nDubhthaigh* (not "Lios an Ufaigh"), meaning "the fort of the O'Duffys" rather than "the fort of the descendant of Fiach." — Library Ireland, "Irish Place Names: Lissonuffy."

[13] **Verified.** "Tulach" means "a little hill" or "hillock." The root word appears in many Irish place names across the country. — Library Ireland, "Irish Place Names: Tulach."

[14] **Verified.** The Ordnance Survey of Ireland was established in 1824. The first survey commenced in November 1824, involving military-trained surveyors gathering geographical, archaeological, and toponymical information. — Wikipedia, "Ordnance Survey Ireland."

[15] **Verified (with precision).** Brian Friel's *Translations* is set in late August 1833 at a hedge school in the townland of Baile Beag, County Donegal. The original text said "the 1830s," which was accurate but imprecise; the specific year is 1833. — Irish Repertory Theatre, "Historical Context for Translations."

[16] **Verified.** John O'Donovan was employed by the Ordnance Survey in 1830 as a field-worker and Names Expert, responsible for entering Irish versions of place names into the Names Books. He systematically compiled historical forms for 64,000 townlands. — John Grenham, "John O'Donovan's glorious letters."
