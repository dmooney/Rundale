# Recent History Before 1820 — What Living Memory Contains

This document covers the historical events that people alive in Kilteevan, County Roscommon in the early 1820s would remember firsthand or have heard about from parents and grandparents. These are not distant abstractions but formative experiences — the stories told at firesides, the names whispered with grief or pride, the betrayals that shaped how ordinary people understood power, loyalty, and survival. For the game, this is the raw material of NPC worldviews: the history that lives in dialogue, in silences, in the way a character reacts to a stranger or a redcoat.

---

## The 1798 Rebellion

The single most shattering political event in living memory for anyone in Connacht in the 1820s is the **1798 Rebellion**. The rising of the **United Irishmen** — a movement born in Belfast in 1791 that sought to unite Catholic, Protestant, and Dissenter under the banner of republican ideals drawn from the French and American revolutions — engulfed much of Ireland in the summer of 1798. [1] Its leader, **Theobald Wolfe Tone**, a Protestant barrister who had sought French military aid, became the symbolic figure of the entire movement, though by the time the rising erupted he was still in France.

For the people of Roscommon, the rebellion was not something that happened elsewhere. In August 1798, a **French expeditionary force of approximately 1,060 soldiers** under General Humbert landed at **Killala** in County Mayo [2] — squarely in Connacht, barely a day's march from Roscommon's western border. The French, joined by thousands of Irish peasants armed with pikes and whatever they could find, marched inland and won a stunning early victory at **Castlebar** on 27 August, routing a much larger British force in what the English derisively called the **"Races of Castlebar."** [3] For a brief, intoxicating moment, a **Republic of Connacht** was declared.

The aftermath was catastrophic. The combined Franco-Irish force was crushed at the **Battle of Ballinamuck** on 8 September 1798, in County Longford [4] — directly neighboring Roscommon, close enough that people in Kilteevan would have heard the cannon or seen the smoke. The French soldiers were taken prisoner and treated as prisoners of war under the conventions of the time. The Irish rebels received no such courtesy. **Mass killings followed the battle itself**, with fleeing pikemen hunted down through the fields and bogs. In the weeks and months after, **courts martial, public hangings, floggings, and house-burnings** swept through Connacht. The punishment was designed not merely to suppress but to terrify. **Transportation to the penal colonies** — Botany Bay, Norfolk Island — claimed hundreds more, tearing men from their families with no realistic hope of return.

In 1820, a man of fifty would have been a young man of twenty-eight during these events. An older woman might remember hiding in the fields while soldiers searched farmsteads. A middle-aged farmer might carry the knowledge that his uncle was hanged from a crossroads tree or that his cousin walked in chains to a transport ship in Dublin. **The 1798 Rebellion is not history in Kilteevan in the 1820s — it is biography.**

## The Act of Union and the Broken Promise

The British government's response to the rebellion was not merely military but constitutional. The **Act of Union of 1800** [5] dissolved the **Irish Parliament** in Dublin — a parliament that, however imperfect and Protestant-dominated, had at least been Irish — and merged Ireland into the **United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland**. Irish representation was reduced to a minority bloc at **Westminster**, where Irish concerns could be comfortably outvoted.

The Act was pushed through partly by bribery and partly by a critical promise: **Catholic emancipation** — the removal of the remaining legal disabilities preventing Catholics from sitting in Parliament, holding senior office, and participating fully in civic life. **William Pitt**, the Prime Minister who engineered the Union, intended to deliver emancipation as part of the settlement. **King George III refused**, citing his coronation oath, and Pitt resigned. [6] The promise was broken before the ink was dry.

This betrayal is fundamental to understanding the political consciousness of Catholic Ireland in the 1820s. The people of Kilteevan live under a constitutional arrangement they did not choose, imposed after a rebellion they were punished for, sweetened by a promise that was never honored. When **Daniel O'Connell** begins his campaign for Catholic emancipation in the 1820s, he is not introducing a new idea — he is demanding the fulfillment of a twenty-year-old debt.

## Robert Emmet's Rebellion (1803)

**Robert Emmet's rising of 1803** was militarily insignificant — a botched attempt to seize Dublin Castle that collapsed into street violence and was suppressed within hours. But Emmet's **speech from the dock** before his execution became one of the most famous utterances in Irish political culture: *"Let no man write my epitaph... When my country takes her place among the nations of the earth, then, and not till then, let my epitaph be written."* [7] By the 1820s, these words had been memorized, repeated, and woven into the fabric of nationalist feeling. Emmet's rebellion demonstrated that the flame of 1798 had not been entirely extinguished, even if his failure also demonstrated its practical hopelessness in the face of British power.

## The Napoleonic Wars and the Agricultural Boom

While rebellion and repression shaped the political memory of the early nineteenth century, the **Napoleonic Wars (1793–1815)** [8] shaped its economic reality. The wars created an enormous demand for grain, beef, butter, and other provisions to feed the British military and its allies. **Agricultural prices soared**, and Irish landlords and farmers alike benefited. Land that might otherwise have lain fallow was brought under tillage. Even smallholders found that they could sell surplus grain at prices that would have seemed fantastical a generation earlier.

This wartime prosperity had profound social consequences. High prices encouraged **early marriage and family formation** — a young couple could reasonably expect to feed children on the returns from even a small plot. Landlords were willing to **subdivide holdings** to maximize the number of rent-paying tenants. The potato, which could feed a family from a smaller acreage than any grain crop, made subdivision viable. The result was a **population explosion**: Ireland's population roughly doubled between 1790 and 1840, rising from approximately four million to over eight million, with the most rapid growth occurring in the western counties. [9] Connacht's population surged, pressing hard against the limits of the land.

**Irish soldiers served in large numbers** in the British army during the Napoleonic Wars. The irony was not lost on anyone: men who might have been rebels in 1798 were wearing red coats at Salamanca and Waterloo. For some families, military service was a path out of poverty; for others, it was a source of shame or ambivalence. Veterans returning to Roscommon after 1815 carried experience of the wider world — and often carried wounds, both physical and psychological, with no pension or support to show for their service.

## The Post-War Crash and Agrarian Unrest

The end of the wars brought economic devastation. After **Waterloo in 1815**, agricultural prices collapsed as continental markets reopened and wartime demand evaporated. The **post-war agricultural depression** hit Connacht with particular severity. Landlords who had expanded during the boom now faced falling rents; their response was often to **consolidate holdings and evict smallholders**. Tenants who had married and subdivided during the good years found themselves trapped — too many people on too little land, with prices too low to sustain them.

The response was **agrarian unrest**. In Connacht, the **Threshers** — a secret agrarian society — emerged in the early 1800s [10], targeting tithe collectors, land agents, and landlords who raised rents or carried out evictions. Their methods included threatening letters, destruction of property, maiming of livestock, and occasionally direct violence against persons. The British authorities responded with the **Insurrection Act**, which granted magistrates sweeping powers to impose curfews, conduct warrantless searches, and transport suspects without full trial. Counties in Connacht were repeatedly **"proclaimed"** under this Act, meaning they were placed under what amounted to martial law.

## Penal Law Relaxation and the Hedge Schools

Against this backdrop of upheaval, the slow relaxation of the **Penal Laws** offered a measure of hope. The **Catholic Relief Acts of 1778, 1782, and 1793** [11] gradually restored the right of Catholics to own property, lease land on longer terms, vote (subject to a property qualification), and establish schools. By the 1820s, the most crushing disabilities of the old penal code had been lifted — but the critical exclusion from Parliament and senior office remained, a daily reminder of second-class status.

The **hedge school tradition** is a living memory in the 1820s. Within the lifetimes of older NPCs, Catholic education was technically illegal, and classes were conducted outdoors, in barns, or in hedgerows — the **hedge schools** [12] where itinerant schoolmasters taught reading, writing, arithmetic, Latin, and sometimes Greek and Irish history to children whose parents risked fines for sending them. By the 1820s, hedge schools had moved indoors and operated more openly, but the memory of persecution shaped attitudes toward learning: education was understood not as a routine entitlement but as something that had been fought for, hidden, and preserved at cost.

## Transportation and the Emotional Landscape

The practice of **transportation** — the sentencing of convicted persons to penal servitude in Australia — haunted rural communities. Transportation was used not only for common criminals but extensively for political offenders and agrarian activists. In a small parish like Kilteevan, the disappearance of even one or two men to the transport ships would have been a communal trauma. Families left behind faced destitution and stigma in some cases, quiet sympathy and support in others, depending on the nature of the offense. The transported were, for all practical purposes, dead to their families — the distance was unimaginable, communication nearly impossible, and return almost unheard of.

The cumulative emotional landscape of Roscommon in the 1820s is one of **layered grief and guarded resilience**. The people of Kilteevan have lived through or inherited memories of rebellion, massacre, betrayal, economic boom and bust, and the slow grinding pressure of a colonial system that alternately punished and ignored them. They have seen promises broken, neighbors taken, and land lost. They have also survived, maintained their language and faith under duress, educated their children in defiance of the law, and built networks of mutual support that functioned beneath and around official structures. Their worldview is shaped by a fundamental awareness that **the system is not theirs** — that the laws, the landlords, the magistrates, and the parliament in Westminster operate according to interests that are not their own.

## Game Design Implications

This history is not background flavor — it is the engine of NPC personality and dialogue. A farmer in his fifties may have **marched with the pikemen at Ballinamuck** or watched his father dragged away for it; that experience will surface when he encounters soldiers, discusses politics, or decides whether to trust a stranger. A widow whose husband was **transported for Thresher activity** will carry that loss into every interaction — her attitude toward authority, toward risk, toward the young men in the parish who might be following the same path.

**Generational trauma** operates at every level. Older NPCs who lived through 1798 will carry direct memories: specific names, specific deaths, specific acts of cruelty or courage. Middle-aged NPCs will have grown up in households shaped by those events, absorbing fear, anger, and caution from parents who had survived the repression. Younger NPCs, born after the rebellion, will know the stories but may feel them differently — as inherited obligation, as romantic legend, or as a warning against futile action.

**Political attitudes** in the parish will not be uniform but will cluster around recognizable positions shaped by this history. Some NPCs will be cautiously hopeful about O'Connell's emancipation campaign; others will be cynical, remembering the broken promise of the Union. Some will regard agrarian agitation as a necessary defense of survival; others will fear it as an invitation to further repression. A few may have quietly prospered under the existing system and will defend it, however awkwardly. These positions should emerge naturally from backstory rather than being assigned arbitrarily.

The **memory of transportation** should function as a persistent undertone in the game world — an ever-present threat that shapes how NPCs talk about risk, law, and resistance. When a character warns another to be careful, they are not speaking in abstractions; they are thinking of a specific person who was not careful and who is now on the other side of the world, or dead.

Finally, the **economic context** — the post-war depression, falling prices, rising rents, the pressure of a growing population on finite land — provides the material foundation for NPC anxieties and conflicts. History in Parish is not merely political; it is felt in the price of oats, the size of a holding, the number of mouths to feed, and the landlord's agent riding into the townland with a ledger and an eviction notice.

## Fact-Check Notes

[1] **Verified with minor correction.** The United Irishmen and the 1798 Rebellion. — The original text said the movement was "born in Belfast and Dublin." The Society of United Irishmen was established in Belfast in 1791 by Theobald Wolfe Tone and others; a Dublin branch followed shortly after. Text amended to specify Belfast as the founding location. Sources: Wikipedia (Irish Rebellion of 1798); The Irish Story.

[2] **Verified with precision added.** French expeditionary force at Killala. — The original text said "over a thousand soldiers." Sources confirm approximately 1,060 French soldiers landed at Kilcummin, near Killala, on 22 August 1798. Text amended to "approximately 1,060 soldiers." Sources: Mayo County Council heritage resources; The Irish Story.

[3] **Verified.** The Battle of Castlebar and the "Races of Castlebar." — Confirmed. On 27 August 1798, a combined force of approximately 800 French and 2,000 Irish rebels routed a British force of 3,000–4,000 under General Lake. The rout was indeed derisively called the "Races of Castlebar." A Republic of Connacht was proclaimed on 31 August with John Moore as president. Sources: Wikipedia (Battle of Castlebar); 1798 Castlebar Festival.

[4] **Verified.** Battle of Ballinamuck, 8 September 1798, County Longford. — Confirmed. The French surrendered and were treated as prisoners of war; the Irish rebels were massacred, with approximately 500 killed and 200 subsequently hanged. Sources: Wikipedia (Battle of Ballinamuck); RTÉ Archives.

[5] **Verified.** The Act of Union of 1800 dissolving the Irish Parliament. — Confirmed. Signed by George III in August 1800, effective 1 January 1801. Ireland was represented at Westminster by 100 MPs and 32 Lords. Sources: Wikipedia (Acts of Union 1800); UK Parliament; History of Parliament Online.

[6] **Verified.** Pitt's promise of Catholic emancipation and George III's refusal citing his coronation oath. — Confirmed. Pitt intended to deliver emancipation as part of the Union settlement. George III refused, reportedly declaring he would consider anyone who proposed it his "personal enemy." Pitt resigned in March 1801. Sources: Britannica (Catholic Emancipation); Victorian Web; History Home.

[7] **Verified.** Robert Emmet's 1803 rising and his speech from the dock. — Confirmed. The rising on 23 July 1803 was a failed attempt to seize Dublin Castle that collapsed into street violence. Emmet's speech at trial on 19 September 1803 included the famous epitaph passage. He was executed on 20 September, aged 25. Sources: Wikipedia (Robert Emmet; Irish rebellion of 1803); Wikisource (Speech from the Dock).

[8] **Verified with note.** The Napoleonic Wars dated 1793–1815. — The dates given encompass both the French Revolutionary Wars (1792–1802) and the Napoleonic Wars proper (1803–1815). Britain entered the conflict in 1793, so the date range is accurate from an Irish/British perspective, though historians technically distinguish the two phases. Sources: Wikipedia (French Revolutionary Wars; Napoleonic Wars); Britannica.

[9] **Corrected.** Ireland's population doubling. — The original text stated population "roughly doubled between 1750 and 1820, rising from perhaps four million to over six million." This conflates the timeline. Ireland's population was approximately 2.5 million in 1750, reaching about 4 million by 1800, and approximately 6.8 million by the 1821 census. The true doubling was from about 4 million in 1790 to 8 million by 1840. Text corrected to "roughly doubled between 1790 and 1840, rising from approximately four million to over eight million." Sources: Wikipedia (Historical population of Ireland); Encyclopedia.com (Population, Economy, and Society from 1750 to 1950).

[10] **Verified.** The Threshers as a secret agrarian society in Connacht. — Confirmed. The Threshers were among several secret agrarian societies (alongside Carders, Rockites, Whitefeet) active in early 19th-century Ireland, primarily targeting tithe collectors and land agents. Sources: Library Ireland; Britannica (Ribbonism); University of Southampton research.

[11] **Verified.** Catholic Relief Acts of 1778, 1782, and 1793 and their provisions. — Confirmed. The 1778 Act restored property and leasehold rights; the 1782 Act abolished mandatory subdivision of Catholic estates and permitted Catholic schools; the 1793 Act restored the parliamentary franchise to Catholic 40-shilling freeholders. Sources: Cambridge University Press; Wikipedia (Roman Catholic Relief Act 1793); Britannica.

[12] **Verified.** Hedge schools as illegal Catholic education conducted outdoors. — Confirmed. Under penal laws from 1695, Catholic education was prohibited. Hedge schools emerged in the early 18th century, teaching reading, writing, arithmetic, Latin, and sometimes Greek. By the end of the 18th century, an estimated 9,000 such schools served 400,000 scholars. By the 1820s they had largely moved indoors. Sources: Wikipedia (Hedge school); Ask About Ireland; History Ireland.
