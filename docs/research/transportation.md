Getting around Ireland in the 1820s was, to put it charitably, an exercise in patience and endurance.

The country had a patchwork transport network that was slowly improving but still largely premodern. Here's how it broke down:

**Walking** was the dominant mode of travel for the rural poor — which was most of the population. Short distances between townlands, to market towns, to church. People walked on bare feet or rough brogues for miles as a matter of course.

**Horse and cart / jaunting car** was the next step up for those with means. The jaunting car — an open two-wheeled side-facing carriage — was distinctly Irish and already ubiquitous by this period. Farmers, small traders, and middling classes used them for local movement.

**Stage coaches** operated on the main routes between larger towns and cities — Dublin to Cork, Dublin to Belfast, etc. They were expensive, uncomfortable by modern standards, and dependent on roads that ranged from tolerable to appalling. Journey times were long — Dublin to Cork could take 12–18 hours.

**The turnpike road system** was expanding in this era. The Grand Jury system (county road boards) was responsible for road maintenance, funded by local taxes, and quality varied enormously by county. The better-funded Leinster roads were considerably more passable than those in Connacht or west Munster.

**Canals** were actually the premium freight and passenger option in the early 1820s. The Grand Canal (Dublin to Shannon) and Royal Canal (also Dublin westward) carried passenger boats called **fly-boats** — lighter, faster barges pulled by horses at trot. Comfortable relative to road travel. The Barrow navigation extended this southward.

**Coastal boats and river traffic** mattered enormously for moving goods and people along the coast — Galway, Cork, Wexford, Drogheda. Much cheaper than road for bulk goods.

**The railways** didn't arrive until 1834 (Dublin–Kingstown, now Dún Laoghaire), so in the 1820s, none of that existed.

One period detail worth knowing for your game: **the state of roads west of the Shannon was genuinely dire** in this period. Richard Griffith's road surveys and the Board of Works improvements mostly came later, in the 1830s–40s. West Connacht and the wilder parts of west Munster were largely cut off from reliable wheeled transport in 1820. People walked, carried goods on their backs or on pack donkeys, and used small boats on loughs and coastal inlets.If you're writing your game set in 1820s rural Ireland, the practical implication is that characters' travel radius is quite limited. A tenant farmer in, say, Roscommon might walk to the nearest market town once a week, travel to a larger town a few times a year, and never see Dublin in their lives. Movement beyond your immediate barony was an event, not a routine.
