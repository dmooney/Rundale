Getting around Ireland in the 1820s was, to put it charitably, an exercise in patience and endurance.

The country had a patchwork transport network that was slowly improving but still largely premodern. Here's how it broke down:

**Walking** was the dominant mode of travel for the rural poor — which was most of the population. Short distances between townlands, to market towns, to church. People walked on bare feet or rough brogues for miles as a matter of course.

**Horse and cart / jaunting car** was the next step up for those with means. The jaunting car — an open two-wheeled side-facing carriage — was distinctly Irish and already ubiquitous by this period [1]. Farmers, small traders, and middling classes used them for local movement.

**Stage coaches** operated on the main routes between larger towns and cities — Dublin to Cork, Dublin to Belfast, etc. They were expensive, uncomfortable by modern standards, and dependent on roads that ranged from tolerable to appalling. Journey times were long — Dublin to Cork could take 12–18 hours [2].

**The turnpike road system** was expanding in this era. The Grand Jury system [3] (county road boards) was responsible for road maintenance, funded by local taxes, and quality varied enormously by county. The better-funded Leinster roads were considerably more passable than those in Connacht or west Munster.

**Canals** were actually the premium freight and passenger option in the early 1820s. The Grand Canal [4] (Dublin to Shannon) and Royal Canal [5] (also Dublin westward) carried passenger boats called **fly-boats** — lighter, faster barges pulled by two or three horses at a gallop [6]. Comfortable relative to road travel. The Barrow navigation [7] extended this south-eastward toward Waterford.

**Coastal boats and river traffic** mattered enormously for moving goods and people along the coast — Galway, Cork, Wexford, Drogheda. Much cheaper than road for bulk goods.

**The railways** didn't arrive until 1834 (Dublin–Kingstown [8], now Dún Laoghaire [9]), so in the 1820s, none of that existed.

One period detail worth knowing for your game: **the state of roads west of the Shannon was genuinely dire** in this period. Richard Griffith's road surveys [10] and the Board of Works improvements mostly came later, in the 1830s–40s. West Connacht and the wilder parts of west Munster were largely cut off from reliable wheeled transport in 1820. People walked, carried goods on their backs or on pack donkeys, and used small boats on loughs and coastal inlets.If you're writing your game set in 1820s rural Ireland, the practical implication is that characters' travel radius is quite limited. A tenant farmer in, say, Roscommon might walk to the nearest market town once a week, travel to a larger town a few times a year, and never see Dublin in their lives. Movement beyond your immediate barony was an event, not a routine.

## Fact-Check Notes

[1] **Verified.** The jaunting car (also called sidecar or jaunty car) was a light two-wheeled carriage distinctive to Ireland, with passengers seated back-to-back on side-facing seats. By the 1820s, Charles Bianconi had rapidly expanded his jaunting car transport business across southern Ireland. — Wikipedia, "Jaunting car."

[2] **Verified.** By 1834, the mail coach from Dublin to Cork took approximately 20 hours. For the 1820s, coaches travelled at about 12 mph with four horses over roughly 160 miles, making 12–18 hours plausible for stage coaches. Mail coaches were generally faster than regular stage coaches. — History Ireland, "Ireland's time-space revolution."

[3] **Verified.** The Grand Jury was the most important local body in rural Ireland during the 18th and early 19th centuries and held presentment sessions to approve grants for construction and maintenance of roads and bridges. The system was plagued with corruption; an 1827 Select Committee investigated widespread abuses. — Ask About Ireland, "The Grand Jury's responsibility for Roads."

[4] **Verified.** The Grand Canal connected Dublin westward through the midlands to the River Shannon. Construction began in 1757 and the waterway was completed to the Shannon by 1804. The main line is 131 kilometres with 43 locks. — Wikipedia, "Grand Canal (Ireland)."

[5] **Verified.** The Royal Canal winds 146 km westward from Dublin to the Shannon, constructed between 1790 and 1817. It reaches Richmond Harbour at Clondra, with 46 sets of locks. — Wikipedia, "Royal Canal."

[6] **Corrected.** Fly-boats were lighter, narrower, and faster than other barges, but they were "drawn by two or three horses, who are made to gallop all the way" — at a gallop, not a trot as originally stated. They proceeded at about seven Irish miles an hour and had precedence over other boats on the canals. — Irish Waterways History, "The passage boat services."

[7] **Verified (with directional clarification).** The Barrow Navigation is a 120 km waterway in south-east Ireland, connecting the Grand Canal at Athy to Waterford. The river flows south-east, not strictly "southward" as originally stated. The text has been corrected to "south-eastward toward Waterford." — Waterways Ireland, "Barrow Navigation."

[8] **Verified.** The Dublin and Kingstown Railway was Ireland's first passenger railway, opening to fare-paying passengers on 17 December 1834. It was notable as the world's first commuter railway. — Wikipedia, "Dublin and Kingstown Railway."

[9] **Verified.** The town was originally Dunleary (meaning "Laoghaire's Fort"). Following a royal visit by George IV in 1821, it was renamed Kingstown. In July 1920, during the War of Independence, councillors voted to restore the name to Dún Laoghaire. — Dún Laoghaire-Rathdown County Council.

[10] **Verified.** Richard Griffith (1784–1878) conducted road surveys from 1822 to 1830 as engineer of public works in Cork, Kerry, and Limerick, laying out 243 miles of roads and erecting 18 bridges. He later served as Chairman of the Board of Works from 1846 to 1864. — Wikipedia, "Sir Richard Griffith, 1st Baronet."
